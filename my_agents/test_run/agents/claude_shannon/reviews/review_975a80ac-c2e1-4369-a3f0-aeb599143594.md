**Venue**: ICLR 2025 (published)  
**Reviewer**: claude_shannon

---

## Summary

MoDeGPT is a training-free LLM compression method that generalizes matrix decomposition from the individual-matrix level to the modular level, jointly decomposing pairs of consecutive weight matrices within each transformer functional unit. The three module types — MLP (up/down/gate), key-query, and value-output — are mapped one-to-one to Nyström approximation, CR decomposition, and SVD, respectively, based on the number of nonlinear functions in each module. This mapping yields bounded reconstruction errors via closed-form algorithms without backward propagation. On LLAMA-2/3 and OPT models, 25–30% compression retains 90–95% of zero-shot performance, with compression time of ~4–8 hours on a single A100 GPU vs. ~1–2 days on 4–8 H100s for the strongest gradient-based competitor. The paper is technically grounded, practically relevant, and well-executed. The main weaknesses are the omission of directly competing SVD-based methods (ASVD, SVD-LLM) from the main tables and an asymmetric error bound for the Type-II module.

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

The prior landscape of LLM structured compression divides into:
1. **Individual SVD**: applies W = AB to each matrix independently, requiring rank < d/2 for true dimension reduction — often collapses accuracy without fine-tuning (Table 3 shows "SVD" yielding perplexities of 52,719 to Overflow at 30–50% compression).
2. **Orthogonal projection (SliceGPT, Ashkboos et al. 2024)**: multiplies by learned orthogonal matrices, reduces dimensions, but adds adapters Q_i^T Q_j on residual paths (~10% parameter overhead).
3. **Gradient-based pruning (LLM Surgeon, LLM-Pruner)**: achieves strong accuracy but requires Hessian/Fisher computations with 4–8 H100 GPUs.

MoDeGPT's contribution is the observation that jointly decomposing two matrices within a module — rather than each independently — enables true dimension reduction without extra adapters, and that the optimal solution for each module type reduces to a classical matrix approximation problem. The type-specific mapping (nonlinearity count → decomposition method) is a genuine theoretical insight:
- Type I (one nonlinearity, MLP): constrained search W_U → W_U S_k links to Nyström approximation of the activation correlation matrix C_σ (Theorem 1).
- Type II (two nonlinearities, K-Q): shared S_k for both W_Q and W_K links to CR decomposition of C_K^{1/2} C_Q^{1/2} (Theorem 2).
- Type III (no nonlinearity, V-O): unconstrained search reduces to standard SVD of C^{1/2} W_V W_O (Theorem 3).

The global sparsity allocation via entropic regularization (Theorem 4, closed-form softmax solution) is a clean auxiliary contribution. The claim that this is "the first method to apply matrix decomposition at the module level for model compression" is accurate to the best of my knowledge.

## Technical Soundness

**Good, with one notable asymmetry.**

**Theorem 1 and Proposition 1 (MLP, Nyström)**: The proof correctly derives that fixing W_U → W_U S_k makes the optimal W_D the Nyström-style reconstruction, and Proposition 1 gives an explicit bound VI ≤ ‖W_D‖²₂ ‖C_σ^{-1}‖₂ ε² d²_int / (k²(1-ε)²) Σ_{i=k+1} σ²_i(C_σ). This is a residual-spectrum bound — it is tight in the sense that it goes to zero as k → d_int.

**Theorem 2 and Proposition 2 (K-Q, CR)**: The bound VII ≤ E²_CR(C_K^{1/2} C_Q^{1/2}) is reasonable, but Proposition 2 gives VII ≤ ((d_h - k)/d_h)² · (Σ σ_i(C_K))(Σ σ_i(C_Q)), which does not depend on the residual spectrum and is thus not tight. For Propositions 1 and 3, the bounds are proportional to Σ_{i=k+1} σ²_i — they go to zero as k increases. Proposition 2's bound does not shrink with k in the same way. This asymmetry is not explained and suggests the CR decomposition has a weaker theoretical guarantee than Nyström or SVD for this module. The paper acknowledges (Section 4.5) that "Type-II has the weakest error bounds" but doesn't clearly explain the asymmetry to the reader.

**Theorem 3 and Proposition 3 (V-O, SVD)**: Optimal for linear modules — the reconstruction error equals exactly Σ_{i=k+1} σ²_i(C^{1/2} W_V W_O), which is the Eckart–Young minimum. Correct.

**Theorem 4 (Sparsity allocation)**: The entropy-regularized optimization over layer sparsities with a sum constraint yields the softmax solution (eq. 11) for sufficiently large ε. This is a standard result in regularized assignment problems; the proof is correct.

**Potential concern**: Algorithm 1 relies on ridge leverage scores (Σ s_i for column selection) from McCurdy (2018). The cited paper is Shannon McCurdy, "Ridge regression and provable deterministic ridge leverage score sampling" (NeurIPS 2018), which does exist and describes the method correctly. Citation is valid.

**Minor inconsistency**: In Algorithm 2 (Type-II), step 3 computes "norm scores" si = ‖C_Q^{1/2}[:, i]‖ · ‖C_K^{1/2}[:, i]‖. This is a heuristic score and does not directly implement the CR decomposition minimizer from Theorem 2, which would select columns of C_K^{1/2} C_Q^{1/2} by norm. The paper does not explain why the factored norm score is preferable to directly scoring columns of the product matrix.

**Largely fair; two notable omissions from main tables.**

Cited in Section 2.1 and Appendix B.5, but only compared in Appendix B.5 against SVD-only approaches. 2. **Missing: SVD-LLM (Wang et al., 2024, arXiv:2403.07378)** — truncation-aware SVD for LLM compression, specifically designed to improve on SliceGPT-style approaches. 3. **Calibration dataset inconsistency in Table 4**: For LLAMA-2 7B rows, "MoDeGPT" uses WikiText-2 calibration while "MoDeGPT-Alpaca" uses Alpaca. The baselines (ShortGPT, SliceGPT, LLM Surgeon) appear to use WikiText-2. Only when Alpaca is used for both (LLAMA-3 8B rows) is the comparison fully aligned. The presentation should clarify whether baselines were also tested with Alpaca calibration.

## Quantitative Analysis

**Table 3 (WikiText-2 perplexity, LLAMA-2)**:
- At 30% compression: MoDeGPT 7B: **7.51** (no gradient) vs. LLM Surgeon 7B: **7.83** (gradient). MoDeGPT is better despite using no backward propagation.
- At 20% compression: MoDeGPT 7B: 6.16 vs. LLM Surgeon 7B: **6.18** (essentially tied); SliceGPT: 7.68.
- At 40%: MoDeGPT 7B: 8.41 vs. LLM Surgeon: 10.39 (MoDeGPT +24% better).
- At 50%: MoDeGPT 7B: 11.88 vs. LLM Surgeon: 15.38.
- 13B results show the same pattern with even larger advantages.

The numbers are internally consistent: gains increase at higher compression rates, consistent with the theoretical claim that modular decomposition is more efficient at capturing structured redundancy.

**Table 4 (zero-shot, LLAMA-2 7B at 30% compression)**:
- Dense: 69.00%
- MoDeGPT (WikiText-2 calibration): 60.78% (–8.22 pp)
- MoDeGPT-Alpaca: 62.02% (–6.98 pp)
- LLM Surgeon: 59.03% (–9.97 pp)
- SliceGPT: 53.73% (–15.27 pp)
- ShortGPT: 53.25% (–15.75 pp)

The improvement over LLM Surgeon (~2pp) is meaningful given that MoDeGPT uses no gradients.

**Table 5 (LLAMA-2 70B at 30% compression)**:
- Dense: 70.02%
- MoDeGPT + our sparsity: 65.51% (–4.51 pp)
- MoDeGPT + our sparsity + Alpaca: 66.79% (–3.23 pp)
- SLEB: 59.79% (–10.23 pp)
- SliceGPT: 52.65% (–17.37 pp)

The margins increase substantially at 70B scale, suggesting MoDeGPT's modular approach scales well.

**Table 6 (compute)**:
- LLAMA-2 7B: MoDeGPT 4h09m on 1×A100 vs. LLM Surgeon 17h08m on 4×H100. At ~2× cost ratio per GPU, this is ~97% cost reduction. Correctly calculated.

**Throughput (Figure 3)**:
- At 50% compression, MoDeGPT achieves +58% throughput while maintaining perplexity at the level of competitors' 30% compression point. This is a clean Pareto improvement claim, though no confidence intervals are reported.

**No confidence intervals or standard deviations reported anywhere.** All results appear to be single-run numbers. For a paper presenting a compression method where calibration data is sampled, reporting variance across different calibration sets would strengthen the empirical claims.

## Qualitative Analysis

The modular decomposition framing is conceptually motivated and experimentally confirmed. The most striking validation comes from Figure 4 and Table 7: MLP dominates parameter budget (66.84% of module parameters), but after normalizing by size, Type-II (K-Q) has the highest "effective ratio r" (0.121 vs. 0.094 for MLP and V-O), meaning K-Q is most sensitive per parameter compressed. This aligns precisely with the theoretical prediction that Type-II has the weakest error bounds. This is a genuine theory-experiment correspondence.

The ablation on calibration size (Figure 5) is practically useful: performance saturates above 128 samples for zero-shot accuracy, even though perplexity continues improving. The 128-sample default is well-chosen.

The sparsity allocation ablation (Table 9) demonstrates that the global softmax allocation outperforms uniform allocation: from 9.06→7.51 ppl (17% improvement) and 53.47%→60.78% zero-shot (7.31 pp). The magnitude of this improvement suggests that layer-specific sensitivity matters substantially — an observation that motivates the global allocation as an important component, not just a detail.

A gap: the paper does not analyze why MoDeGPT's advantage over competitors grows with model scale (7B → 13B → 70B). The theoretical analysis is presented at a fixed layer width and does not explain scale-dependent behavior.

## Results Explanation

**Provided explanations**:
- Why Type-II is most sensitive: weakest error bound (Proposition 2 vs. 1,3) — confirmed by experiment. Clear.
- Why MoDeGPT outperforms standalone SVD: module-level optimization expands the search space — stated in Section 3.1.
- Why Alpaca calibration outperforms WikiText-2: distribution closer to downstream tasks — stated in Section 4.3.

**Unexplained**:
- Why does MoDeGPT's advantage grow at larger model scales? The 70B results show much larger gaps vs. baselines. No explanation is offered.
- Why does Algorithm 2 use factored norm scores rather than direct column-norm scoring of the product C_K^{1/2} C_Q^{1/2}? This implementation detail deviates from the direct implication of Theorem 2.
- The "effective rank r" in Table 7 is defined as the fraction of the rank budget actually used (0.094, 0.121, 0.095 for Types I, II, III). This is listed but not interpreted: why do these values converge to ~10% of the rank budget? No explanation.

## Per-Area Findings

### Area 1: Module-level decomposition framework (weight: 0.65)

**Prior work landscape**:
- SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024): closest structural competitor. Reduces dimensions via orthogonal projection; adds ~10% parameter adapters. MoDeGPT correctly identifies this overhead as a limitation.
- ASVD (Yuan et al., 2023): activation-aware SVD per matrix. Does not do joint decomposition. Correctly cited but not experimentally compared.
- SVD-LLM (Wang et al., 2024): truncation-aware SVD with whitening. Direct competitor. Cited but neither discussed nor compared.

**Findings**: The joint decomposition framework is genuinely novel and theoretically grounded. The three-way mapping (nonlinearity count → decomposition type) is a clean organizing principle. The experimental results convincingly show improvement over standalone SVD and SliceGPT. The omission of ASVD and SVD-LLM from main tables is a weakness, particularly since SVD-LLM explicitly addresses the limitations of individual matrix SVD that MoDeGPT targets. Without that comparison, the claim of "state-of-the-art structured compression via low-rank decomposition" is not fully substantiated.

### Area 2: Theoretical error bounds (weight: 0.35)

**Findings**: Theorems 1–4 are mathematically correct and internally consistent. The theory-experiment alignment (Type-II weakest by theory → most sensitive by experiment) is a genuine validation. However, Proposition 2 (Type-II bound) is substantially weaker than Propositions 1 and 3: it does not shrink proportionally to the residual spectrum as k increases, meaning there is no theoretical guarantee that increasing the K-Q rank budget improves the bound in the way that increasing MLP or V-O rank does. This asymmetry is not discussed and leaves the Type-II compression less theoretically principled than the other two modules.

## Synthesis

**Cross-cutting themes**:
- Both the theory and experiments consistently identify Type-II (K-Q) as the bottleneck module. This alignment between theory and practice is the paper's strongest feature.
- The no-gradient requirement is the unifying practical constraint, and MoDeGPT delivers on it effectively across all model scales tested.

**Tensions**:
- The paper claims "state-of-the-art structured compression" but excludes two directly competing SVD-based methods (ASVD, SVD-LLM) from main comparisons. If those methods are worse, including them would only strengthen the paper; their absence raises a question about selective comparison.
- Proposition 2's weaker bound for Type-II is in tension with the overall narrative of "provably bounded errors" for all three module types.

**Key open question**: How does MoDeGPT compare to ASVD and SVD-LLM at the same compression rates on the same benchmarks? These are the most directly competing methods and their absence from the main paper is the primary unresolved question.

## Open Questions

1. **Why are ASVD and SVD-LLM not in the main tables?** Both are published before the paper's submission and are directly comparable. An Appendix B.5 comparison with a subset of these methods is insufficient for a claim of state-of-the-art.

2. **Why does MoDeGPT's advantage grow at larger scales?** The 70B result shows 65.51% vs. SLEB's 59.79% (5.72 pp gap) while the 7B result shows 60.78% vs. LLM Surgeon's 59.03% (1.75 pp gap). A scaling analysis would strengthen the practical case.

3. **Does Proposition 2 have a tighter formulation?** The current K-Q bound does not decrease with k in the way that bounds for Type-I and Type-III do. Is this a fundamental limitation of CR decomposition for this module, or can a tighter analysis recover a residual-spectrum bound?

4. **What is the sensitivity to ridge intensity λ?** Algorithm 1 requires choosing λ for leverage score computation. No sensitivity analysis is provided.

5. **Can MoDeGPT be combined with quantization?** The paper focuses on structured decomposition but modern deployment often combines pruning/decomposition with quantization. A brief experiment (e.g., MoDeGPT + GPTQ) would clarify practical deployment value.

---

## Final Assessment

MoDeGPT presents a well-motivated and theoretically principled approach to structured LLM compression. The modular decomposition framework is genuinely novel, the three-way mapping to classical matrix approximations is elegant, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate improvements over SliceGPT and comparable performance to gradient-based methods at a fraction of the compute cost. The paper's main weaknesses are the omission of two directly competing methods (ASVD, SVD-LLM) from main tables, the weaker theoretical guarantee for the Type-II module, and the absence of variance estimates across calibration runs. These are meaningful but not fatal flaws for an ICLR paper — the contribution is real and the execution is solid.

**Score: 7/10**
