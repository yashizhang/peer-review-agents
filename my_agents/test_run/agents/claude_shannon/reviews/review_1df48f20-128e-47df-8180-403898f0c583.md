# Review: Linearly Controlled Language Generation with Performative Guarantees

**Paper ID:** 1df48f20-128e-47df-8180-403898f0c583
**Venue (submitted):** ICLR 2025 (under double-blind review)

---

## Summary

This paper proposes LiSeCo (Linear Semantic Control), a controlled language generation method that combines linear probing of LM latent space with an optimal control framework to steer text away from disallowed semantic regions (toxicity, negativity, formality) at inference time. The core theoretical contribution is a closed-form optimal steering vector (Theorem 1), derived via KKT conditions on a per-layer constrained minimization, that is guaranteed (in probability) to keep latent trajectories outside a linearly defined disallowed region while minimizing the L2 perturbation to the original activation. The method requires a supervised probe training phase, then applies zero-gradient, closed-form interventions at each layer during generation. Experiments cover toxicity and negativity avoidance across Llama-3-8B, Mistral-7B, and Pythia-6.9B on RealToxicityPrompts and a sentiment task dataset, with comparisons against ActAdd, FUDGE, and instruction-tuned models. The theoretical framing is genuinely novel — this is the first activation steering approach to provide formal probabilistic guarantees, not merely empirical steering — and the closed-form solution is both elegant and practically useful. However, the empirical case has notable gaps: small evaluation sample sizes (N=25–37 for per-model toxic continuations), a naturalness evaluation conducted by the authors rather than independent raters, the theoretical guarantee not holding for Llama and Pythia in practice (only Mistral), and absence of comparison against DExperts, LEACE, and other contemporary activation-steering baselines.

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

The framing of activation-steering as a constrained optimization problem solvable in closed form is genuinely novel. Prior work (ActAdd/Turner et al. 2023, ITI/Li et al. 2024a, REMEDI/Hernandez et al. 2023) provides empirical steering without guarantees or requires backpropagation at inference time (Plug and Play/Dathathri et al. 2019). Soatto et al. (2023) apply control theory to LMs but do not derive performative guarantees validated in practice, as the paper correctly notes. The two theorems (Theorem 1 for toxicity-constraint-as-hard-constraint, Theorem 2 for toxicity-minimization-with-size-constraint, Corollary 1 for continuous tuning, Corollary 2 for range control) together form a coherent mathematical framework that goes well beyond prior empirical steering work. The linear representation hypothesis (Park et al. 2023, 2024) as a foundation is a reasonable and increasingly well-supported assumption. The limitation is that the "guarantee" is contingent on linear probe quality — which the paper demonstrates is imperfect (probe-external classifier alignment is only significant for Llama and Mistral, not Pythia) — so the guarantee is theoretical in a weak sense when probes fail to generalize. This is honestly disclosed.

## Technical Soundness

The mathematical derivation in Theorem 1 (Appendix C) is correct. The KKT proof proceeds correctly: the Lagrangian is formed (Eq. C.4), the stationarity, complementary slackness, primal, and dual feasibility conditions are applied systematically, and the closed-form solution (Eq. C.15) follows rigorously. One concern: the paper factorizes the binary sigmoid classifier as σ(W^T x) with W ∈ R^{d×2}, but the actual derivation uses the difference of columns w_t := W^1_t - W^2_t and reduces to a scalar logistic condition. The step from Eq. C.9 to C.10 is correct but glosses over the derivation of the binary cross-entropy reduction. This is minor but could be made clearer. Theorem 2 (naturalness-first) and Corollaries 1 and 2 are stated with proofs that are correct by inspection — they follow analogously from Theorem 1.

The claim that local per-layer guarantees compose into a global guarantee on the generated token is asserted but not proven with full rigor. The argument (Section 4.2) appeals to linearity of the unembedding matrix and monotonicity of softmax, which is correct in principle, but the composition of local guarantees across layers is nontrivial because layer t+1 depends on the modified state x̃_t, and interventions at layer t may push x̃_{t+1} into a disallowed region at layer t+1 that was previously not activated. The paper acknowledges this by saying "locally optimal" rather than globally optimal, but does not prove that the per-layer composition is sound in the worst case. This is a meaningful theoretical gap.

The relaxation from problem (1) to problem (2) (dropping the layer-coupling constraint 1c) is correctly motivated by non-convexity of global DP, but the guarantee for problem (2) is strictly weaker than for (1) and this should be stated more prominently.

## Baseline Fairness Audit

**Toxicity / Negativity Experiments:**

Baselines include no-control, instruction-tuning (Llama-instruct, Mistral-instruct), ActAdd (Turner et al. 2023), and FUDGE (Yang & Klein, 2021). The comparison is reasonable but incomplete:

- **DExperts** (Liu et al., 2021) is cited but not included as a baseline, despite being a direct competitor in the decoding-based steering space. It is notably absent from the experimental comparison.
- **LEACE** (Belrose et al., 2023) is cited as related work but not compared. LEACE performs linear concept erasure in closed form from the same linear-representation foundation as LiSeCo — this is the most directly analogous prior work and its omission is conspicuous.
- **SelfDeBias** (Schick et al., 2021) is cited but not compared.
- The ActAdd comparison uses a single (source, target) prompt pair per task — ("toxicity"→"kindness"), ("optimism"→"despair") — with a grid search over layer l ∈{6, 15, 24} and strength c ∈{0.01, 0.1, 1, 3, 9, 15}. The best setting is reported (c=0.1, l=6 for toxicity). This is a reasonable effort, but ActAdd's performance is highly dependent on prompt choice, and testing only a single prompt pair may underrepresent the method's capability.
- FUDGE is used with k=50 (top-k tokens scored per step), which the paper treats as a "privileged" upper-bound baseline because it directly optimizes the ground-truth scorer. This framing is fair.

**Evaluation protocol concern:** The toxicity evaluation is restricted to a randomly sampled "label-balanced" set of N=25–37 would-be-toxic continuations per model (described in Section 6). This is a very small sample. Results from N=25 cannot support strong statistical conclusions, and no confidence intervals or significance tests are reported in Figure 3 or 4 (only ±1 SD ellipses in Figure 3).

## Quantitative Analysis

**Inference latency (Table 1):** LiSeCo adds 0 to 0.013s overhead per token (negligible) vs. FUDGE at 3.41–3.56s per token — a 2–3 order of magnitude reduction. This is the cleanest quantitative result in the paper and strongly supports the efficiency claim.

**Toxicity reduction (Figure 4 bottom, would-be toxic continuations):**
- Llama: LiSeCo p=0.01 reduces toxicity from 100% to 29% (instruction-tuned: 17%, ActAdd: 44%, FUDGE: 14%)
- Mistral: LiSeCo p=0.01 reduces toxicity from 100% to 25% (instruction-tuned: 7%, ActAdd: 75%, FUDGE: 14%)
- Pythia: LiSeCo p=0.01 reduces toxicity from 100% to 34% (no instruct, ActAdd: 50%, FUDGE: 7%)

Key observations:
1. LiSeCo with p=0.01 reduces toxicity substantially but does not outperform instruction tuning for Llama (29% vs. 17%) or Mistral (25% vs. 7%). FUDGE outperforms LiSeCo for all models.
2. For Pythia, LiSeCo p=0.01 achieves 34% toxic, which is worse than ActAdd's 50% appears to be — wait, Table values show Pythia FUDGE at 7%, LiSeCo p=0.01 at 34%. This means FUDGE is substantially better on Pythia.
3. The theoretical guarantee (probe probability p should upper-bound toxic percentage) holds empirically only for Mistral: for Llama at p=0.01, 29% > p=0.01, and for Pythia at p=0.01, 34% >> p=0.01. The paper acknowledges this failure and attributes it to probe-external alignment quality (Spearman ρ=0.06 for Pythia, not significant; ρ=0.20 for Llama; ρ=0.38 for Mistral).

**Naturalness:** Evaluated by the authors on a Likert scale 1–5 in "blind setup" (Appendix N). This is a significant methodological weakness — author-rated naturalness is subject to motivated reasoning, even in blind setups. No inter-annotator agreement is reported. Human naturalness ratings did not correlate with perplexity (acknowledged in footnote 3), which is itself concerning.

**Probe accuracy (Figure 2):** Probes reach ~90% validation accuracy for mid-layers across models for both toxicity and sentiment tasks. This is clearly sufficient to motivate the method, but the 90% accuracy means probes regularly misclassify both directions — the false positive rate (flagging non-toxic text as toxic) is not separately reported.

## Qualitative Analysis

Table K.1 shows generated text examples for the toxicity task. The examples confirm the qualitative behavior claimed: LiSeCo (p=0.01) on Llama produces natural continuations ("on our website that we've received from our readers..."), while ActAdd with c≥1 produces degenerate repetitive token sequences. The false-positive intervention failure mode is also shown — for Mistral on a non-toxic prompt, LiSeCo p≤0.1 produces degenerate text. This is the most important qualitative finding: LiSeCo can suppress generation quality when the probe fires on non-toxic inputs. The paper acknowledges this ("false positive intervention") but does not characterize the rate of false positives systematically.

The formality tuning (Appendix F) shows that LiSeCo is monotonic in p across the formality dimension (Figure F.2), which is a useful demonstration of the continuous tuning formulation. However, the range covered is narrow (implied human rating ~0 to 1.5 out of a [-3, 3] scale), suggesting the method cannot achieve extreme formality shifts.

## Results Explanation

The paper provides good mechanistic explanations for key findings:
- Why p predicts toxicity only for Mistral: explained by probe-external classifier alignment (Spearman ρ)
- Why perplexity does not correlate with naturalness: attributed to degenerate outputs with artificially low perplexity (ActAdd at high c)
- Why probe scores decrease for instruction-tuned models: confirms linear probes capture toxicity semantics transferably
- The theoretical guarantee failure for Llama and Pythia: attributed to probe generalization gap

What remains unexplained:
- Why does LiSeCo produce false positive interventions for Mistral on non-toxic inputs (Figure 3, center, p=0.01)? The probe accuracy is ~90% for Mistral, so this should occur infrequently — yet the degenerate outputs in Table K.1 suggest the false positive rate is non-negligible in practice.
- The sentiment experiments (Figure 3 bottom) show that LiSeCo performs less well than toxicity in terms of naturalness preservation. No explanation is offered for why sentiment control degrades naturalness more than toxicity control.

## Open Questions

1. **Why is LEACE not compared?** LEACE (Belrose et al., NeurIPS 2023) provides closed-form linear concept erasure and is theoretically the most similar method to LiSeCo. Its absence from comparison significantly weakens the paper's empirical case.

2. **Author-conducted naturalness ratings:** Appendix N (human evaluation instructions) is referenced but not included in the reviewed submission. How were annotators blinded to method identity given that the authors themselves conducted ratings? What was inter-annotator agreement?

3. **False positive intervention characterization:** When LiSeCo fires on non-toxic prompts (as shown in Table K.1 for Mistral, p≤0.1), what is the false positive rate across the full evaluation set? How does this affect naturalness scores?

4. **Compositionality of per-layer guarantees:** The global guarantee is claimed from local per-layer guarantees via linearity of unembedding and monotonicity of softmax. Is this composition formally correct when θ_t at layer t changes the input to layer t+1? Does the intervention at layer t propagate to make Rt+1 more likely to be violated?

5. **Li et al. 2023a/2024a and Meng et al. 2022a/2022b duplications:** are these intentional double-citations or bibliographic errors?

6. **Scaling to larger models:** LiSeCo adds O(T×d) probe parameters (one per layer, linear). For Llama-3-8B with 32 layers and d=4096, this is ~131k parameters per probe — negligible. But the paper tests only 6–9B parameter models. How does the method scale to 70B+ models where d is much larger and more layers may fire simultaneously?

7. **Greedy decoding only:** All experiments use max 50 tokens, greedy decoded. How does LiSeCo behave with sampling-based generation or nucleus sampling? Greedy decoding is not standard for naturalness evaluation and may inflate naturalness scores.

## Per-Area Findings

### Area 1: Theoretical Framework and Closed-Form Guarantee (Weight: 0.55)

The KKT-based derivation of the optimal steering vector is mathematically clean and verifiable. The closed-form solution (Theorem 1, Equation 3) is elegant: θ*_t is proportional to the probe weight vector w_t with a magnitude that depends on how far the current state is from the decision boundary. This formulation is the first in the activation steering literature to provide a formal guarantee. Theorems 2, Corollaries 1 and 2 extend the framework to naturalness-first and continuous tuning settings, demonstrating genuine mathematical generality. The main theoretical limitation is the compositionality argument for global guarantees from local ones (Section 4.2), which is asserted rather than proved, and the strong dependence of the guarantee on linear probe quality, which is empirically demonstrated to fail for Pythia and partially for Llama.

### Area 2: Empirical Validation on Toxicity/Negativity/Formality (Weight: 0.45)

The experimental section validates the theoretical predictions qualitatively: p does predict toxicity reduction direction, probe scores do constrain to < p for Mistral, and latency is negligible vs. FUDGE. These are the core empirical claims and they hold. The limitations are: (a) sample sizes of N=25–37 for the primary per-model analysis are too small for strong conclusions; (b) LEACE and DExperts are absent from comparison; (c) naturalness evaluation by the authors without reported inter-annotator agreement is methodologically weak; (d) greedy-only generation limits generalizability; (e) the formality experiment is preliminary (one model, Llama only) with narrow dynamic range.

---

## Synthesis

**Cross-cutting themes:** The paper's strongest contribution is theoretical — a clean closed-form guarantee that is new to the steering literature. The empirical work validates the theory directionally but has methodological weaknesses (sample sizes, author ratings, greedy-only decoding, missing LEACE baseline) that prevent confident quantitative conclusions.

**Tensions:** There is a tension between the strong theoretical claims (guaranteed in probability) and the empirical finding that the guarantee fails for 2 of 3 models tested. The paper handles this tension honestly by attributing it to probe quality, but it weakens the practical force of the guarantee claim.

**Key open question:** LEACE (Belrose et al., 2023) performs closed-form linear concept erasure from the same theoretical foundation. How does LiSeCo compare to LEACE on the same toxicity/negativity tasks? This comparison would most directly establish whether the optimal-control framing provides any advantage over the erasure framing, both in terms of steering strength and text naturalness.

## Literature Gap Report

1. **LEACE** (Belrose et al., NeurIPS 2023) — Perfect linear concept erasure in closed form. Cited in the paper but not compared experimentally. This is the most directly analogous method and its omission is the primary gap.
2. **DExperts** (Liu et al., ACL 2021) — Decoding-time controlled text generation with expert and anti-expert LMs. Cited in related work but not compared experimentally.
3. **CAD / Contrastive Decoding** (O'Brien & Lewis, 2023; Li et al., 2023) — Contrastive decoding approaches for steering generation. Not cited.
4. **RepE** (Zou et al., NeurIPS 2023) — Representation engineering for LM control, applies linear probing in a steering context directly comparable to LiSeCo. Not cited or compared.
