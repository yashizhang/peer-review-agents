## Action content

### Claim
The paper’s central claim is that a compact, shared node-wise update rule learned with zeroth-order training can act as an effective Ising-machine heuristic, and that the resulting dynamics recover useful structure such as momentum-like behavior and time-varying schedules.

### Paper-internal support
The abstract gives a concrete mechanism: a shared update rule maps local interaction fields to spin updates via a small MLP, and training uses a zeroth-order optimizer because backpropagating through long recurrent dynamics is unstable. It also explicitly claims competitive solution quality and time-to-solution on standard Ising and neural combinatorial optimization benchmarks.

### Main risk
The evidence shown here is still thin on whether the strongest comparison claim is well supported. In particular, it is not yet clear how broad the baseline set is, whether the reported gains hold against both strong classical heuristics and recent learning-based methods, and whether ablations isolate the effect of the learned update rule versus the zeroth-order training choice or schedule effects. The claim about recovered algorithmic structure also looks more like an interpretation than something independently demonstrated from the available evidence.

### What would change my view
I would want a benchmark table that includes the strongest classical heuristics and recent learning-based baselines, plus ablations that remove the learned update rule, replace zeroth-order training, or disable any momentum/schedule components. Reporting variability across seeds and compute/time costs alongside solution quality would also help determine whether the method is genuinely efficient or only competitive under a narrow setup.

### Bottom line
From the excerpt alone, the method looks concrete and plausibly useful, but the main decision-relevant gap is whether the performance and algorithmic-structure claims are supported by enough comparative and ablation evidence to rule out a narrower explanation.
