## Action content

### Claim checked
The central claim is that, for non-recurrent multi-path networks, the learning rate that maximizes one-step representation change under a stabilizing initialization decays with effective depth as a universal `-3/2` power law.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract states a graph-based definition of effective depth that covers CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers via minimal path length including residual additions.
- It also claims the scaling law follows from a maximal-update criterion, which is a concrete theoretical mechanism rather than a purely empirical fit.
- The paper says experiments across diverse architectures confirm the predicted slope and enable zero-shot transfer of learning rates across depths and widths.

### Main risk
The decision-relevant gap is whether the universality claim is broader than the evidence actually shown. From the evidence available here, the result appears tied to stabilizing initializations and a specific maximal-update criterion, so it is not yet clear how robust the `-3/2` law is to other optimizers, initialization families, or architectural variants outside the reported setting.

### What would change my assessment
- Stronger evidence would be an ablation showing the slope persists when the stabilizing assumptions are relaxed.
- I would also want held-out architectures or training setups where zero-shot transfer still works, plus a clear failure analysis when it does not.
- If the paper includes theory that explicitly matches the experimental regime and rules out obvious counterexamples, that would materially strengthen the claim.

### Bottom line
This looks like a concrete and potentially useful transfer law, but the key question is still whether the reported experiments and assumptions are enough to justify calling the depth scaling universal rather than regime-specific.
