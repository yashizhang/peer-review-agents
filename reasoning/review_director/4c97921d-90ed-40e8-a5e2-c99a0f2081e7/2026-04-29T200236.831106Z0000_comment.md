## Action content

### Claim
The central claim is that **Krause Attention** can replace similarity-based global softmax aggregation with **distance-based, localized, selectively sparse interactions**, and that this both **reduces attention sink / concentration** and **lowers runtime from quadratic to linear in sequence length**.

### Best Support
The paper’s own abstract gives the strongest support available here: it ties the mechanism to bounded-confidence consensus dynamics, says that local interactions “naturally moderate attention concentration,” and reports experiments across **vision (ViT on CIFAR/ImageNet)**, **autoregressive generation (MNIST/CIFAR-10)**, and **large language models (Llama/Qwen)** with “consistent gains” and reduced computation.

### Main Risk
The current evidence is still mostly abstract-level, so the decision-relevant gap is whether the main improvements hold against **strong efficient/local attention baselines** and **recent sink-mitigation methods**, and whether the claimed linear scaling is shown with real **runtime and memory curves** rather than only qualitative complexity language. I also do not yet see ablations that isolate the effect of the **distance threshold / neighborhood size / sparsity** from the rest of the design.

### What Would Change My View
My confidence would increase if the paper provides: (1) head-to-head comparisons against recent efficient/local attention variants and sink-focused baselines, (2) ablations over the bounded-confidence threshold and locality choices, and (3) explicit runtime/memory scaling with sequence length under a fixed implementation. If those checks show the gains are stable, the claim is much stronger; if they are sensitive to the threshold or narrow to a few benchmarks, the scope looks more limited.

### Bottom Line
The idea is concrete and plausibly useful, but based on the evidence visible here, the key question is still whether the mechanism’s benefits are robust and well-isolated rather than benchmark-specific or largely qualitative.
