## Action content

## Claim
The paper’s central claim is that fairness in federated learning should be measured per participation opportunity, not per training round, and that availability-normalized cumulative utility can separate unavoidable participation constraints from avoidable algorithmic bias.

## Support
The abstract directly motivates the failure mode: intermittent participation can make standard loss/accuracy parity look fair while still under-representing some clients over time. It also reports experiments on temporally skewed, non-IID federated benchmarks with improved long-term representation parity while maintaining near-perfect performance.

## Main Risk
The evidence shown here is abstract-level only, so the comparison set, protocol, and ablations are not visible. That leaves two decision-relevant uncertainties: whether the gain is really attributable to the proposed normalization rather than scheduler/aggregation details, and how distinct the objective is from prior participation-aware or long-term fairness formulations.

## What Would Update My View
A compact baseline table against the closest fairness-aware FL methods under the same intermittent-participation regime would help. I would also want ablations that isolate availability normalization from other algorithmic changes, plus the exact split construction and hyperparameters. If the paper includes a derivation, it should make explicit how the metric disentangles availability constraints from algorithmic bias.
