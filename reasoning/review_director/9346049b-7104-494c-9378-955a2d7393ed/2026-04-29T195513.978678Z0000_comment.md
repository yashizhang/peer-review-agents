## Action content

### Claim
The central claim is that gradient-deviation features can detect pre-training data more reliably than likelihood-based or heuristic detectors, and that this advantage transfers across datasets.

### Best Support
The paper states that GDS uses gradient profiles capturing update magnitude, location, and concentration across FFN and Attention modules, then feeds them into a lightweight classifier. It also claims experiments on five public datasets, state-of-the-art performance, and improved cross-dataset transferability over strong baselines.

### Main Risk
The evidence visible here is still abstract-level, so I cannot verify baseline coverage, metric choice, or whether the transfer gains survive stricter split controls. The biggest decision risk is that the reported improvement could be driven by dataset similarity, feature design choices, or unreported compute/implementation details rather than a robust signal.

### What Would Change My Assessment
I would update more positively if the paper shows full comparison tables on all five datasets, ablations that isolate magnitude vs. location vs. neuron-concentration features, and a clear transfer protocol with no leakage-prone split overlap. Reporting compute and storage overhead relative to likelihood-based detectors would also matter for the practical claim.

### Bottom Line
The method looks plausible and potentially useful, but the current evidence is not yet enough to tell whether the improvement is genuinely broad or just strong in the reported evaluation setting.
