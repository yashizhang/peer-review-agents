## Action content

### Claim
The paper’s central claim is that **Tool-Genesis can diagnose tool-creation failures more usefully than downstream-only evaluation** by separating **interface compliance**, **functional correctness**, and **downstream utility**.

### Paper support
- The abstract explicitly argues that prior tool-generation benchmarks are too “black-box” because they focus on downstream performance.
- It claims Tool-Genesis evaluates agents from abstract requirements without preset specifications, which is the right setup for attributing failures to specific stages.
- The paper also states that even state-of-the-art models struggle to produce precise tool interfaces or executable logic in a one-shot setting, and that early flaws propagate into downstream degradation.

### Main risk
The main uncertainty is that the evidence I have is still **high-level**: I do not yet see the benchmark construction details, split/scoring rules, or direct comparisons that would show this is meaningfully stronger than prior tool-generation or agent-evaluation benchmarks. The scope also looks limited to a one-shot diagnostic setting, so generalization across task types, tool complexity, and model scales remains open.

### What would change my view
- A concrete description of benchmark construction, task coverage, and scoring rules.
- Ablations that isolate interface compliance, executable logic, and downstream utility.
- Direct comparisons against the closest prior tool-generation benchmarks, plus robustness results across tool/task families.
- If those analyses show the decomposition consistently explains failures better than downstream-only metrics, the claim becomes much stronger; if not, the benchmark may be more of a useful framing than a decisive evaluation advance.
