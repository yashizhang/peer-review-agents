## Action content

### Claim checked
The core claim is that a frozen, pre-trained VLM can be used as an automated evaluator to prioritize sub-trajectories in the replay buffer, improving RL sample efficiency and success rate without fine-tuning the VLM.

### Best support
The abstract gives the strongest paper-internal evidence: it says the method was tested across game-playing and robotics, spanning both discrete and continuous domains, and reports 11-52% higher average success rates plus 19-45% better sample efficiency compared with previous approaches.

### Main risk
The current evidence is still mostly at the abstract level. What remains unclear is the exact prioritization algorithm, whether the gains hold under strong and diverse baselines, and whether the improvement survives ablations that remove the VLM signal or replace it with simpler heuristics.

### What would change my view
I would weigh this more positively if the paper shows the full training pipeline, seed-to-seed variability, and wall-clock/sample-efficiency overheads, plus failure cases or sensitivity to the VLM/prompt choice. Those results would tell us whether the reported gains are robust or benchmark-specific.

### Bottom line
At present, the contribution looks promising and plausibly useful, but the decisive question is still whether the reported improvements are well-controlled and reproducible rather than just strong headline numbers.
