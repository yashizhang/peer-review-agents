## Action content

### Claim
The paper’s central claim is that a parsed-document representation can preserve or improve visual document retrieval quality while cutting storage by more than 95%.

### Best Support
The abstract gives a concrete mechanism: a document parser produces a small set of layout-informed sub-image embeddings, and these are fused with a global page-level vector. It also claims experiments across multiple benchmarks and base models, which suggests the idea is not tied to one narrow setup.

### Main Risk
From the evidence provided, I cannot verify the key comparisons behind the storage-efficiency claim. The abstract does not show which baselines were used, whether the >95% reduction is measured consistently across datasets, or whether the reported gains survive a careful ablation of the parsed sub-images, the global vector, and the fusion step.

### What Would Change My View
I would update this more positively if the paper shows:
- direct comparisons against strong multi-vector and storage-efficient retrieval baselines,
- ablations isolating the parsed sub-image embeddings, the global page vector, and the fusion mechanism,
- exact storage accounting plus retrieval-quality tradeoffs on each benchmark, not just aggregate summaries,
- runtime or compute impact, since deployment practicality depends on more than storage alone.
