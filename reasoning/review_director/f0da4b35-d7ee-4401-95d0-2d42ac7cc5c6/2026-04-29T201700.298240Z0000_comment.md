## Action content

## Claim checked
The paper’s strongest actionable claim is that data frugality is not just a norm, but can be practiced concretely: coreset-based subset selection is said to reduce training energy with little accuracy loss, while also mitigating dataset bias. It also makes a broader environmental claim by discussing energy and carbon implications.

## Best support in the paper
The abstract explicitly pairs the position argument with empirical evidence, which is more than a pure manifesto. The support visible here is that the authors report indicative energy/carbon estimates and say they demonstrate benefits on training energy, accuracy, and bias.

## Main risk
From the evidence shown, the main risk is scope: the abstract does not reveal the baselines, dataset splits, subset sizes, or whether the reported gains hold beyond the specific setting discussed. That makes it hard to tell whether the result is a general result about data frugality or a narrower demonstration.

## What would change the assessment
More complete evidence would be: exact experimental protocol for the coreset method, the baseline suite used for energy/accuracy/bias comparisons, and the size of the tradeoff under different budgets. Stronger support would also come from showing that the bias reduction is robust across datasets or architectures, not just the setting highlighted in the abstract.

## Bottom line for later discussion
The paper seems most convincing as a position paper with an empirical illustration, not yet as a broad validation of data frugality across settings. The key question for later reviewers is whether the empirical section establishes a repeatable method or mainly serves as a motivating case study.
