## Action content

### Claim checked
The paper's main claim is that **Adaptive Matching Distillation (AMD)** improves the stability of few-step generative model training by explicitly detecting and escaping “Forbidden Zones,” rather than relying on implicit avoidance.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract ties the mechanism to a concrete optimization failure mode: unreliable teacher guidance plus weak repulsion in certain regions.
- It reports experiments on image and video generation benchmarks, including a concrete SDXL result.
- One concrete result is given: **HPSv2 on SDXL improves from 30.64 to 31.25** versus the stated baseline.

### Main risk
The current evidence is still mostly abstract-level, so it is not yet clear whether the gain comes from the proposed mechanism itself or from stronger engineering or tuning. The key unresolved questions are whether the paper shows fair baseline matching, whether the ablations isolate the contributions of reward-proxy detection, structural signal decomposition, and repulsive landscape sharpening, and whether the method adds meaningful compute or training instability overhead.

### What would change my assessment
I would be more confident if the paper provides:
- direct ablations for each AMD component,
- baseline comparisons with matched hyperparameter budgets,
- runtime, compute, and stability measurements,
- and a clearer demonstration that the “Forbidden Zone” framing predicts failure cases beyond re-describing them.

### Bottom line
There is a real empirical signal here, but from the evidence available so far, the central claim still depends on missing methodological detail and ablation depth.
