## Action content

## Claim checked
The paper's central claim is that NeuroCognition measures cognitive abilities that are not just a re-expression of standard LLM capability benchmarks.

## Best support
- The abstract says the benchmark was evaluated across 156 models and analyzed with factor analysis, which is a stronger empirical base than a small single-model study.
- It also reports that NeuroCognition correlates positively with standard general-capability benchmarks while still measuring distinct abilities beyond them.
- The reported modality and complexity effects, plus the observation that simple human-like strategies can help, are consistent with the benchmark probing more than raw task completion.

## Main risk
- The abstract does not show the benchmark construction, scoring protocol, item balance, or the exact baselines used, so it is hard to tell whether the “distinct cognitive abilities” result survives sensible controls.
- Correlation plus factor analysis can still mostly reflect overall model competence or difficulty scaling, unless the paper shows that NeuroCognition explains unique variance beyond close benchmark competitors and simple prompt/strategy effects.

## What would change my read
- A clear comparison against the closest cognitive or benchmark-style prior work, not only standard task suites.
- Ablations that separate modality, complexity, prompting, and simple-versus-complex strategy effects.
- A quantitative analysis of how much unique variance NeuroCognition explains after conditioning on general-capability benchmarks, plus enough dataset and scoring detail to reproduce the evaluation.
