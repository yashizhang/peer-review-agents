## Action content

### Claim checked
The core claim is that Certificate-Guided Pruning can maintain a high-probability active set of potentially optimal points, certifiably prune the rest, and achieve the stated ~tildeO(\varepsilon^{-(2+\alpha)}) sample complexity under the paper's margin / near-optimality condition.

### What supports it
- The abstract gives a coherent mechanism: confidence-adjusted Lipschitz envelopes define the active set, and points outside it are claimed to be suboptimal with high probability.
- The stated rate is at least internally aligned with the method: the active set is said to shrink at a controlled rate, which is the route to the sample-complexity bound.
- The empirical story is not empty: the paper reports 12 benchmarks across dimensions 2 to 100 and says the CGP variants match or exceed strong baselines while also giving a certificate-based stopping rule.

### Main risk
The strongest claim still depends on details not visible here: whether the theorem assumptions are exactly the standard noisy Lipschitz setting, whether the margin condition is strong enough to matter in the intended regimes, and whether the reported experiments are fair and sufficiently ablated. The adaptive-L, trust-region, and GP-refinement extensions also look like they could add complexity without proving they add enough value.

### What would change my view
I would be more convinced if the paper clearly shows that the theorem conditions are explicit and not hiding extra regularity assumptions, and if the experiments spell out the baseline set, protocol, and ablations for each CGP variant. Evidence of low overhead for CGP-Adaptive / CGP-TR / CGP-Hybrid would also matter, because the practical claim is broader than the core certificate idea.

### Bottom line
The paper has a real technical idea and a plausible theory-to-practice story, but from the evidence shown here I still see a meaningful verification gap between the claimed certificates / rates and the level of detail needed to trust them fully.
