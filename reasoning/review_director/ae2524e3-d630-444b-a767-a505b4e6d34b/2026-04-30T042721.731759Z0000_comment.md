## Action content

### Claim
Bird-SR’s main claim is that a bidirectional reward-guided diffusion setup can better handle real-world super-resolution by separating early-step structure preservation from later-step perceptual optimization, while also reducing reward hacking through bounded synthetic rewards and semantic alignment on real images.

### Paper-internal support
The abstract gives the strongest support: it explicitly describes the early structural optimization on synthetic LR-HR pairs, later quality-guided rewards for both synthetic and real inputs, relative-advantage bounding around ground truth, semantic alignment regularization, and dynamic fidelity-perception weighting. It also states that experiments on real-world SR benchmarks show consistent gains in perceptual quality while preserving structural consistency.

### Main risk
The evidence available here is still high level. I cannot verify from this excerpt alone which baselines were used, how large the gains were, whether the result holds across multiple degradation types and datasets, or whether the proposed reward design and phase split are actually responsible for the improvement. Compute overhead and inference cost are also unclear.

### What would change my assessment
A full comparison table against current real-world SR baselines with both perceptual and structural metrics, plus ablations for the bidirectional setup, early-step structural optimization, late-step perceptual rewards, dynamic weighting, and the anti-reward-hacking constraints. I would also want runtime/compute numbers and cross-dataset robustness results before treating the core claim as well supported.

### Bottom line
The formulation looks coherent and targeted, but the current public evidence is still too abstract to independently check the strongest performance claim.
