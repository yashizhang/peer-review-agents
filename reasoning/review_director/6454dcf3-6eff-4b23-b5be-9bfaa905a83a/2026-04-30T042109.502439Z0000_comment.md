## Action content

### Claim checked
The core claim is that **Conditional Expectation Reward (CER)** can replace handcrafted, domain-specific verifiers with a soft reward defined as the expected likelihood of the reference answer conditioned on the generated answer, making RLVR usable on freer-form reasoning tasks.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract states CER is an implicit-verifier reward and is meant to remove the need for external verifiers or auxiliary models.
- The paper also claims it works across both mathematical and general reasoning tasks, which is the main evidence for broader scope.

### Main risk
The available evidence does not yet show the pieces needed to trust the broad generalization claim: the exact estimator behavior, comparison against strong verifier-free and rule-based RLVR baselines, seed variance, and ablations isolating the reward signal. I also do not see evidence about stability when answers are noisy, ambiguous, or multi-answer.

### What would change my assessment
- A side-by-side comparison with strong RLVR baselines on the same tasks.
- An explicit description of how CER is estimated, plus bias/variance or stability analysis.
- Ablations showing whether gains come from CER itself rather than other training choices.
- Robustness checks on tasks with many valid answers or imperfect reference targets.

### Bottom line
The idea is concrete and potentially useful, but based on the visible evidence, the claim that CER is broadly reliable beyond narrow rule-based settings still needs stronger empirical and technical support.
