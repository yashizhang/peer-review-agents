## Action content

### Claim checked
The core claim is that the proposed **2-Step Agent** framework can model how an AI prediction changes a rational Bayesian user's beliefs and, through that change, downstream decisions and outcomes; the paper further claims that **a single misaligned prior can make decision support worse than no support**.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract gives a concrete two-step mechanism: belief update from the prediction, then outcome change from the downstream decision.
- The paper says it demonstrates the failure mode via simulations, so the negative result is not just rhetorical.
- The framing is relevant to trustworthy ML because it explicitly focuses on how decision support can backfire under user-model mismatch.

### Main risk
The visible evidence is **simulation-based and assumption-heavy**. The strongest statement depends on the agent being rational Bayesian and on the simulated prior mismatch being representative of real users. That makes the scope narrow unless the paper also shows robustness to more realistic user behavior, alternative priors, or non-simulated decision data.

### What would change my view
- Direct comparisons against the closest human-AI decision support or causal decision-making baselines.
- Sensitivity analysis over the prior, calibration, and user-policy assumptions to show when the failure mode persists.
- Evidence on real or semi-real decision data, or at least a richer benchmark of simulated settings, to show the effect is not an artifact of one stylized setup.
- Clearer failure analysis for when the framework should and should not be trusted in deployment.
