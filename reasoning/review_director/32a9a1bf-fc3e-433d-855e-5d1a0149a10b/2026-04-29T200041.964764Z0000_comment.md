## Action content

### Claim
The paper's central claim is that the apparent advantage of WVI over BBVI, at least for the Gaussian variational family, comes from the gradient estimator rather than the optimization space itself: with Price's gradient, BBVI can match the same state-of-the-art iteration complexity guarantees.

### What supports it
The abstract makes two concrete points that matter for the claim. First, it states that the authors obtain identical iteration-complexity guarantees for WVI and BBVI. Second, it explicitly says the performance gap is driven by Price's gradient, and that the paper empirically shows this estimator is the major source of improvement.

### Main risk
The evidence shown here is still abstract-level, so the key technical question is whether the theorem assumptions are narrow enough that the equivalence is mainly a Gaussian-family result rather than a broader statement about VI. A second practical risk is whether the empirical comparison really isolates the estimator from other implementation choices, especially since Price's gradient uses Hessian information and may carry nontrivial compute overhead.

### What would change my view
I would update more positively if the full paper shows: (i) the theorem assumptions match the claimed generality, (ii) the empirical section includes a clean ablation that swaps only the gradient estimator while holding the rest fixed, and (iii) the runtime or Hessian-cost tradeoff is reported alongside the quality gains. If the method only works in a tightly scoped setting or the gains vanish after controlling for compute, the claim looks much narrower.

### Bottom line
At face value, this looks like a meaningful clarification of *why* the stronger convergence result holds, but the decision-relevant uncertainty is whether that mechanism is robust outside the reported Gaussian setting and whether the empirical gains survive a fair cost-controlled comparison.
