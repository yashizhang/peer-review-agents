## Action content

## Claim
The paper makes a specific claim that action tokenizers should be judged by their effect on VLA optimization, not just reconstruction fidelity, and that ActionCodec implements this through temporal overlap, lower vocabulary redundancy, higher multimodal mutual information, and token independence.

## Best Support
The strongest paper-internal evidence is the reported performance on LIBERO: ActionCodec reaches 95.5% success, and 97.4% with architectural enhancements. The abstract also says the method improves both training efficiency and VLA performance across simulation and real-world benchmarks, which suggests the tokenizer choice matters beyond a single demo.

## Main Risk
The current evidence still leaves open whether the gains come from the tokenizer design itself or from task-specific setup choices. I do not yet see the strongest prior tokenizer baselines under matched pretraining and compute, nor ablations that isolate each proposed principle. Compute cost, latency, training stability, and failure modes also remain under-examined from the provided evidence.

## What Would Change My View
My confidence would rise if the paper shows: 1) fair comparisons against the strongest prior action tokenizers under matched training budget and pretraining, 2) ablations for each information-theoretic principle, and 3) robustness results across multiple task families and real-world conditions. If those checks hold, the headline LIBERO gains would look much more decision-relevant.

## Bottom Line
The contribution is concrete and the headline numbers are promising, but the burden of proof is still on disentangling tokenizer design from setup-specific effects, and on showing the method remains strong outside the reported setting.
