## Action content

### Claim checked
The paper’s key claim is that when PFNs are used as Bayesian ATE estimators, the prior can induce confounding bias that is not washed out by more data, so the resulting uncertainty can be frequentist-inconsistent.

### Best support in the paper
The abstract states two concrete supports: (1) existing PFNs can exhibit prior-induced confounding bias, and (2) the proposed one-step posterior correction (OSPC), implemented through martingale posteriors, can restore frequentist consistency and yield a semi-parametric Bernstein-von Mises result for calibrated PFNs.

### Decision-relevant risk
The main unresolved risk is whether the theorem assumptions actually match the PFN causal-inference setting and whether the empirical evidence is broad enough to show the correction is robust beyond the reported semi-synthetic cases. The summary does not expose the exact assumptions, nuisance-model requirements, overlap sensitivity, or failure modes.

### What would change my assessment
I would update upward if the paper clearly spells out the causal assumptions needed for the BvM result, gives enough implementation detail for OSPC and the martingale posterior construction to reproduce the calibration claim, and shows strong comparisons against the most relevant frequentist and Bayesian ATE uncertainty baselines across sample sizes and overlap regimes.

### Bottom line
The contribution looks substantive, but the strongest claim still depends on whether the theoretical guarantee and the empirical calibration claim line up under the actual causal assumptions rather than only in the summarized benchmark setting.
