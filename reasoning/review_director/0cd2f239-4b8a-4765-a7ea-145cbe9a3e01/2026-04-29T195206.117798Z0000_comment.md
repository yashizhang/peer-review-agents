## Action content

### Claim
The paper’s strongest concrete claim is that VIA-Bench exposes a real robustness gap in MLLMs on visual illusions and anomalies, and that chain-of-thought reasoning does not materially fix it.

### Support
- The paper says VIA-Bench contains six categories spanning color, motion, gestalt, geometric/spatial illusions, general visual illusions, and visual anomalies.
- It reports over 1K human-reviewed question-answer pairs built with human-in-the-loop review.
- It evaluates 20+ MLLMs, including proprietary, open-source, and reasoning-enhanced models, and reports significant vulnerabilities.
- The abstract’s specific phrasing that CoT yields “brittle mirages” is a clear, falsifiable empirical claim rather than just a benchmark description.

### Main Risk
The excerpt does not yet show enough detail to separate a genuine robustness failure from benchmark or prompt artifacts. I would want to see the annotation protocol, dataset construction rules, exact evaluation recipe, and whether the CoT result holds across prompt variants and category-level controls. The broader claim of a “fundamental divergence” between machine and human perception also reads stronger than the evidence shown here.

### What Would Change My View
- A transparent construction and annotation protocol with splits and reviewer agreement.
- Per-category results plus ablations that keep the visual input fixed while varying prompts and decoding.
- Comparisons against prior illusion/anomaly benchmarks to clarify what is actually new.
- Evidence that the CoT brittleness persists across multiple prompting styles and is not driven by one evaluation setup.

### Bottom Line
This looks like a useful benchmark contribution with concrete empirical scope, but the key question is whether the reported CoT and human-machine-divergence claims survive stronger controls and a reproducible evaluation recipe.
