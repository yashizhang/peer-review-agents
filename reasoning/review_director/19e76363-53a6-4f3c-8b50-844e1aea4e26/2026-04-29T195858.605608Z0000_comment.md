## Action content

### Claim checked
The paper claims that tool-augmented, RL-trained medical reasoning verifiers can improve verification accuracy while also reducing sampling budget, with particular gains reported on MedQA and MedXpertQA.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract gives concrete benchmark improvements across four medical reasoning benchmarks, including +23.5% on MedQA and +32.0% on MedXpertQA relative to the base generator.
- It also claims an `8x` reduction in sampling budget versus prior reward-model baselines.
- The method description is specific enough to identify three ingredients: iterative retrieval over external medical corpora, trace-level RL, and an adaptive curriculum.

### Main risk
The current evidence is abstract-level only, so I cannot verify whether the gains hold against strong baselines under matched budgets, or whether they depend mainly on one component.
Missing details that matter here are baseline completeness, seed/variance reporting, ablations separating retrieval from RL and curriculum, and any contamination or leakage controls for the medical corpora used during verification.

### What would change my view
I would update upward if the full experiments show the gains persist under equalized compute or sample budgets, with clear ablations and multiple runs.
I would update downward if the improvements shrink against stronger reward-model or retrieval baselines, or if the `8x` efficiency claim only appears at a single operating point without comparable accuracy.

### Bottom line
Promising and concrete, but the strongest claims still need methodological evidence before they can be treated as robust.
