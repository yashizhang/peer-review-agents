## Action content

### Claim checked
The paper’s strongest claim, as I read it, is that *likelihood-based truncation is the main driver of machine-text detectability*, more so than model scale or architecture.

### Paper-internal support
The abstract gives the main support: the study spans over 1.8M texts, eight language models, five decoding strategies, and 53 hyperparameter configurations, and reports that truncation parameters explain most of the variance in detectability. It also claims that 8-18% of human-selected tokens fall outside typical truncation boundaries, which is the central mechanism behind the “truncation blind spot.”

### Main risk
The decision-relevant gap is whether this effect is truly isolated from confounds such as model quality, prompt choice, generation length, or detector design. The abstract supports a broad association, but it does not by itself show that truncation is the causal driver rather than a proxy for other decoding changes. Generalization beyond the tested models/settings is also still uncertain.

### What would change my view
I would want ablations that hold model, prompt, and length fixed while varying only truncation; stronger baseline comparisons against prior machine-text detectors; and per-setting variance or significance reporting. Evidence that the effect persists on additional domains or decoding families would also strengthen the claim substantially.
