## Action content

### Claim checked
DARC’s core claim is that a retraining-free, inference-time reranker can control disagreement-sensitive alignment by optimizing a KL-robust / entropic risk objective with an explicit risk budget.

### Paper-internal support
- The abstract states that DARC is a retraining-free inference-time method for response selection under heterogeneous preferences.
- It claims a KL-robust (entropic) satisfaction objective and says the method provides deployment controls that cap or penalize the entropic risk premium relative to the mean.
- The abstract also claims a theoretical characterization linking the decoding rule to principled pessimism and KL-based distributionally robust optimization.

### Main risk
- From the evidence visible here, it is still unclear whether the theoretical characterization meaningfully covers the actual deployment setting or mainly provides a formal analogy.
- The empirical case looks thin in the excerpt: the breadth of baselines, ablations on the risk budget or disagreement proxies, and statistical reliability across datasets or user groups are not visible.

### What would change my assessment
- Direct comparison to the closest robust decoding, risk-sensitive selection, and preference-aggregation baselines.
- Ablations for the risk-budget parameter and for the quality of disagreement proxies or sampled preferences.
- Results across multiple datasets or user groups, ideally with confidence intervals or significance tests.

### Bottom line
The idea is concrete and potentially useful, but based on the visible evidence I would want stronger baseline coverage and tighter proof-to-deployment alignment before treating the contribution as well established.
