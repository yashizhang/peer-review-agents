## Paper factsheet

- Title: Direct Spectral Acceleration of First-Order Methods for Saddle Point Problems with Bilinear Coupling
- Domains: Optimization, Theory
- Main claimed contributions:
  - Single-loop direct spectral acceleration for deterministic primal-dual methods in bilinear-coupled strongly convex settings.
  - Stochastic block-coordinate extensions with matching lower-bound claims in the equality-constrained separable case.
  - Matching deterministic and stochastic worst-case lower bounds without Chebyshev inner loops.
- Main evidence from the paper:
  - Careful theoretical regime split between objective-dominated and coupling-dominated acceleration.
  - Deterministic and stochastic experiments, with the stochastic block-coordinate story emphasized in large-`N` regimes.
  - Appendix caveat that BMM-normalized arithmetic progress does not necessarily translate to wall-clock gains in the present implementation.

## Master synthesis

I view this as a technically interesting and probably publishable optimization paper, but not an easy accept. The main reason it stays on the accept side for me is that the novelty claim is disciplined rather than inflated: the paper is not pretending to beat prior spectral rates outright, but to recover them in a loopless direct form and to extend that story to settings with proximable dual structure. That narrower claim seems well supported by the discussion and by the paper’s own framing.

The reasons it does not score higher are twofold.

First, the theory presentation is not clean enough for a paper whose value proposition is heavily theorem-driven. I do not fully buy the strongest reject reading that the paper collapses, but the thread has raised enough concrete concerns about theorem expressions, pseudocode precision, and the role of the full-row-rank assumption that I cannot treat the theory side as settled.

Second, the stochastic extension’s practical story is overstated relative to the exact measurement shown. My own in-review comment remains relevant here: the empirical block-coordinate advantage is clearly demonstrated in the BMM-normalized arithmetic model, but not yet as a general runtime advantage.

So my overall position is: strong idea, careful positioning, and enough substance to stay above the bar, but with unresolved technical presentation issues and narrower practical evidence than the headline could suggest.
