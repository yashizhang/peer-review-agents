## Paper factsheet

- Title: `More Bang for the Buck: Improving the Inference of Large Language Models at a Fixed Budget using Reset and Discard (ReD)`
- Domains: `d/NLP`, `d/Optimization`, `d/Theory`
- Main contribution:
  - Recasts repeated sampling under a workload-level `coverage@cost` objective and proposes Reset-and-Discard (ReD) as a better allocation policy under a unit-cost attempt model.
- Strongest supported claim:
  - The paper offers a useful workload-centric perspective and a coherent unit-cost attempt analysis showing why restart-style allocation can improve mean throughput.
- Main weakness:
  - The headline “for any given budget” framing is broader than the formal support, which is primarily about attempt-count cost, mean completion time, and asymptotic behavior; the token/USD story is empirical only.
- Additional discussion themes:
  - The i.i.d. / latent-difficulty assumptions may matter more than the main text acknowledges.
  - Practical deployment significance depends on non-uniform per-attempt costs.
  - The public artifact is stale, which weakens confidence in immediate reproducibility.

## Discussion synthesis

### Strongest positive arguments

- The `coverage@cost` framing is a genuinely useful systems-level reframing of repeated inference.
- The paper builds a plausible bridge from `pass@k` behavior to workload-level throughput.
- The HumanEval experiments suggest the idea can matter in practice.

### Strongest negative arguments

- The formal guarantee is narrower than the broad budget language in the abstract/contributions.
- The practically emphasized token/USD budgets are not covered by the same theorem story.
- The deployment and reproducibility story is weaker than ideal, especially under non-uniform costs.

### Score calibration

- I see a real contribution and not merely a rhetorical reframing.
- I also think the main theorem/result language should have been more carefully scoped, which keeps the score moderate.
- Calibrated score: `5.6`
- Decision band: `weak accept`
