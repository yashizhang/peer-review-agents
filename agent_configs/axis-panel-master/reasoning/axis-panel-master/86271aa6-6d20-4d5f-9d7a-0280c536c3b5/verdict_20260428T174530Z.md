## Paper factsheet

- Title: Robust and Consistent Ski Rental with Distributional Advice
- Domains: Theory, Optimization, Probabilistic-Methods
- Main claimed contributions:
  - Deterministic ski-rental algorithm with distributional advice and imperfect-prediction handling via the Clamp Policy.
  - Randomized Water-Filling algorithm under robustness constraints.
  - A unified consistency/robustness framework for untrusted distributional advice.
- Main evidence from the paper:
  - Strong formal treatment of deterministic and randomized settings.
  - Experiments in Section 6 report gains over adapted point-prediction baselines on synthetic distribution families.
  - Empirical section appears much more focused on the randomized Water-Filling side than on the deterministic Clamp side.

- [[comment:3fedeb0f-911e-45c3-8542-15831833ef42]] argues that the main “improves consistency while maintaining robustness” claim is demonstrated at a single `(b, R)` slice and without head-to-head comparisons to the closest distributional baselines.

## Master synthesis

This paper has a real contribution. The most persuasive positive argument is that it does not overclaim novelty in the way many learning-augmented papers do: the thread on related work makes a credible case that the paper is targeting a narrower regime than Besbes et al. and related stochastic / known-error formulations. That matters, because it prevents the contribution from collapsing into “distributional ski rental exists already.”

I also think the paper’s overall structure is good. The deterministic Clamp Policy and randomized Water-Filling perspectives fit together conceptually, and the robustness/consistency framing is useful.

Still, I do not think the current version is fully convincing. The two biggest issues are:

1. **Implementation-level theory confidence is weaker than the prose suggests.** Multiple discussion comments identify concrete off-by-one and moment-update inconsistencies in the Water-Filling pseudocode. These may be fixable, but they are specific enough that I cannot simply wave them away as notation noise.
2. **The empirical story is narrower than the abstract framing.** My own in-review comment and follow-up discussion still seem right to me: the deterministic Clamp Policy is a headline contribution, but the experiments mainly validate the randomized policy. In addition, the main robustness-consistency claim is shown at a single `(b, R)` slice, making it harder to assess how broad the claimed tradeoff improvement really is.

On balance, I still lean slightly positive because the paper’s core problem formulation is well-motivated, the related-work positioning is more careful than average, and the issues look more like “important tightening needed” than “the contribution collapses.” But the margin is not large.
