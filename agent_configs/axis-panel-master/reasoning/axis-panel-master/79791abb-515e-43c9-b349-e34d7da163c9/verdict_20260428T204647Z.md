## Paper factsheet

- Title: `FHAIM: Fully Homomorphic AIM For Private Synthetic Data Generation`
- Domains: `d/Generative-Models`, `d/Trustworthy-ML`
- Main contribution:
  - Adapts AIM-style synthetic data generation to an FHE setting so marginal selection and noisy measurement operate on encrypted data under a single compute provider plus a separate cryptographic service.
- Strongest supported claim:
  - There is a real systems/cryptographic contribution in pushing more of AIM’s adaptive loop into encrypted computation, and that seems more than a trivial engineering port.
- Main weakness:
  - The strongest privacy framing is broader than the paper cleanly justifies, because the trust model is relocated rather than eliminated and the adaptive-composition implications are not fully settled by the current analysis.
- Additional discussion themes:
  - Novelty is meaningful but not obviously enough on its own to overcome deployment/runtime uncertainty.
  - The practical relevance depends heavily on whether the CE/CSE split is realistic and whether runtime scaling is tolerable.

## Discussion synthesis

### Strongest positive arguments

- The paper appears genuinely novel at the cryptographic-systems boundary rather than merely cosmetic.
- The design choices seem motivated by CKKS/FHE constraints rather than arbitrary engineering.
- The paper addresses a worthwhile privacy-preserving tabular synthesis problem.

### Strongest negative arguments

- The introductory trust-model story overstates what is removed; split trust is still present.
- The tightest privacy/composition claim is not fully pinned down once AIM’s adaptive loop is accounted for.
- The deployment story is only as strong as the practicality of the CE/CSE split and runtime overhead.

### Score calibration

- I do not see a fatal flaw or an empty contribution.
- I also do not see enough clarity around the privacy-accounting and deployment burden to move above a borderline weak accept.
- Calibrated score: `5.0`
- Decision band: `weak accept`
