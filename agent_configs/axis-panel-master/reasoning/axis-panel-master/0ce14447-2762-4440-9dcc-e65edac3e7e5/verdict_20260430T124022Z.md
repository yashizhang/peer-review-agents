## Paper Factsheet

- Domains: d/Theory, d/Optimization.
- Main contribution: the paper argues that trained neural-network sign matrices are close to iid Rademacher-like, difficult to compress, and mostly inherited from initialization; it formalizes this with a stopping-time sign-lock-in theory and proposes gap initialization plus outward-drift regularization.
- Main evidence: Figure 2 and Appendix diagnostics compare sign-matrix spectra/entropy/compressibility to Rademacher baselines; Section 3 gives the stopping-time theory; Section 4 and Appendix language/vision experiments test gap and outer-drift interventions.
- Claimed practical bridge: Appendix "Approximately Zero-Cost Sign Template Method for Sub-Bit Compression" fixes sign templates, then stores only magnitudes via SVD-like factors, with bit accounting for the zero-template and baselines.
- Scale evidence: Appendix "Billion-Scale LLM Validation" trains character-level Transformers from 31M to 12.9B parameters on Tiny Shakespeare with sequence length 64, micro-batch size 1, 1000 optimizer steps, AdamW8bit, constant learning rate 3e-4, and 2048 sampled scalar coordinates per tracked tensor.
- Theory-to-optimizer caveat: the appendix remark says the theorem needs bounded updates plus re-entry control; scheduled-SGD gives a sufficient condition, while Adam/AdamW would require controlling the preconditioned momentum step and a lower bound on the adaptive denominator.
- Practical-compression caveat: Appendix G.3 uses template-aware gap initialization and an elementwise hard projection after each optimizer update to enforce the template signs exactly, which differs from unconstrained natural sign persistence.

## Sub-Agent Outputs

### Evidence Completeness Agent

Axis score: 4/10
Accept/reject signal: weak reject
Confidence: medium

#### Strongest evidence

- The paper gives broad diagnostics for sign incompressibility and sign persistence across Transformers, CNNs, and MLPs.
- The gap/outer-drift experiments report multi-seed trends and connect empirical estimates to the theory's initial-hit and re-entry factors.

#### Main concerns

- The largest-scale validation is not a standard LLM-pretraining regime: Tiny Shakespeare, 1000 steps, micro-batch 1, and sparse coordinate sampling make it a controlled dynamics probe rather than deployment-scale evidence.
- The strongest sub-bit compression result uses fixed sign templates and hard projection, so it demonstrates a constrained sign-template method rather than showing that natural lock-in alone makes signs free.
- Missing passive baselines such as initialization-seed plus XOR/residual encoding weaken the practical compression claim.

#### Missing checks that would change the decision

- A direct PRNG-seed plus XOR-mask baseline with entropy coding and matched bit accounting.
- A longer, realistic pretraining-scale sign-flip study.
- A comparison of gap/outer-drift alone versus hard projection under the same bit/performance budget.

## Master Synthesis

This is a technically interesting paper with a real observation: trained sign patterns often remain close to their initialization signs and are hard for generic compression methods to exploit. The stopping-time framing is useful, and the paper is more than a superficial empirical note. However, the applied claim that this resolves a practical sub-bit "one-bit wall" is not yet load-bearing. The largest-scale validation is a short Tiny Shakespeare character-LM dynamics sweep, the AdamW bridge is acknowledged but not verified, and the strongest compression evidence uses fixed sign templates plus hard projection. The missing passive differential-sign baseline is especially important because it exploits exactly the low-flip phenomenon while preserving the original training path. I therefore score the paper as a borderline but below-accept weak reject rather than a clear reject.
