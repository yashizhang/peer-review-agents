## Paper Factsheet

The paper proposes cumulative utility parity (CUP) for fair federated learning under intermittent client availability. The core idea is to measure client benefit per participation opportunity rather than per round, using availability-normalized cumulative utility, adaptive sampling based on inverse availability, and representation-aware surrogate corrections for unavailable clients.

Main paper evidence read:
- Abstract and Introduction: the paper claims CUP improves long-term representation parity while maintaining near-perfect performance.
- Section 2 / 3: Lemma 1 and Theorem 1 define availability-normalized utility and variance-based parity; Lemma 2 claims inverse-availability sampling equalizes long-run selection frequency to m/N; Theorem 2 introduces missed-count reactive weights.
- Section 3.1: utility is introduced as a loss-reduction style quantity.
- Section 4 / Table 2: baseline comparisons report utility CV and Jain utility metrics, with text indicating retrospective utility computation for baselines.
- Appendix A / Eq. 40: the available convergence-style bound grows with T rather than decaying.

## Sub-Agent Outputs

### Evidence Completeness Agent
Axis score: 3/10

The paper addresses an important fairness gap and includes experiments on temporally skewed federated settings. However, the core fairness metrics in Table 2 are hard to trust because the discussion identifies non-comparable utility definitions between the proposed method and baselines. The empirical setup does not rescue the theoretical problems because the claimed metric improvements depend on exactly the disputed utility construction.

### Clarity and Reproducibility Agent
Axis score: 4/10

The motivation is understandable, but the utility definition and its use in experiments are not sufficiently consistent. The paper does not make it clear that all methods are evaluated with a common post-hoc utility definition, and the relationship between randomized inverse-availability sampling in the proof and deterministic top-K selection in the method/implementation is unclear.

### Practical Scope Agent
Axis score: 4/10

The fairness problem is practically relevant for FL systems with intermittent participation. The proposed method's scope is not yet established because the theoretical selection rule does not appear to achieve the desired parity and the reported fairness gains may be metric artifacts.

### Technical Soundness Agent
Axis score: 2/10

The central formal claims are not sound as written. Lemma 2 replaces a random denominator in the inverse-availability sampling probability by its expectation/limit inside an expectation. A two-client availability example breaks the claimed equal selection probability. Lemma 1 and the appendix bounds also appear to require restrictive equal-utility assumptions or yield a bound that grows with the horizon.

### Novelty and Positioning Agent
Axis score: 5/10

The fairness framing is useful: participation-opportunity-normalized utility is a reasonable lens distinct from per-round loss parity. But the paper's acceptability depends on whether the formal and empirical apparatus supports that lens, and here it does not.

## Master Synthesis

The paper has a good problem statement but fails on the load-bearing technical claims. The major issue is not a minor proof gap: if inverse-availability sampling does not equalize expected selections and the implemented selector is a different top-K mechanism, then the route from CUP motivation to guaranteed scheduling behavior breaks. The empirical comparison then has a second independent weakness: the utility metrics appear not to be computed on the same utility definition across methods. With both theory and the central empirical fairness metric compromised, this is a clear reject despite a useful framing.
