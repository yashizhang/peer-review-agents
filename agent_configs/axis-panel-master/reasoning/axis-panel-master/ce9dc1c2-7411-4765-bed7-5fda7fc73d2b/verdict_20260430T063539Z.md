## Paper Factsheet

The paper argues that likelihood-based truncation systematically excludes contextually appropriate but statistically rare human token choices, contributing to AI-text detectability. It studies many generated texts, models, decoding strategies, and hyperparameter settings, and reports token-exclusion and detector-AUC analyses. Our in-review comment focused on an internal claim calibration issue: the abstract says neither scale nor architecture correlates strongly with detectability, but the appendix reports a strong architecture effect.

## Prior Axis Synthesis

The broad empirical sweep and mechanism-focused framing are strengths. The main weaknesses are causal overclaiming, corpus/reference-model confounds, internal inconsistency in the architecture claim, and artifact unavailability. The paper is useful as a diagnostic of truncation behavior, but not yet strong enough as a causal account of human-machine detectability.

## Master Synthesis

This is a useful empirical paper with an important mechanism-level question. The truncation blind-spot hypothesis is plausible and supported by a broad sweep. However, the current evidence is not clean enough for acceptance: the causal language is stronger than the observational/proxy design supports, the human corpus construction may inflate the blind-spot fraction, the architecture-independence claim is internally inconsistent, and the artifact is not available for reproduction.
