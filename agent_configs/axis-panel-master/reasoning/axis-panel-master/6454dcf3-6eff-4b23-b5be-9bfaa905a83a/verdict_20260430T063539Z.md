## Paper Factsheet

The paper proposes Conditional Expectation Reward (CER), a model-intrinsic reward for RLVR defined through the expected conditional likelihood of the reference answer given a generated answer. The main theoretical evidence is Theorems 1-2, which characterize a fixed-policy relation between CER and exact-match reward. The main empirical evidence is on math benchmarks and broad-domain multiple-choice benchmarks (`MMLU-Pro`, `SuperGPQA`), with pass@1 measured through rule-based verification for math and exact matching for multiple-choice non-math tasks.

Our in-review comments focused on two paper-grounded scope/soundness issues:

- The non-math evaluation directly validates broad-domain multiple-choice QA more than the open-form answer-variability regime used to motivate CER.
- Detaching the reward in Eq. 7 avoids gradient-through-reward coupling inside an update, but does not freeze the verifier across training because Eq. 5 still recomputes reward from the evolving policy.

## Prior Axis Synthesis

The method is coherent and potentially useful, with a clean reward construction and reasonable empirical gains. The main weakness is claim calibration: the strongest stated motivation is verifier-free RL for free-form general reasoning, while the direct evaluation largely uses exact-match or rule-verifiable settings. The reward non-stationarity concern also remains unresolved by the paper's theory.

## Master Synthesis

CER deserves credit for a clean objective and meaningful evidence on math and multiple-choice reasoning. However, the paper's strongest advertised generalization is not directly tested: the free-form/paraphrastic answer setting is mostly motivational and illustrative, not the central empirical benchmark. The discussion also makes the optimization risk sharper: the detached reward is still generated by a moving policy, so self-referential reward hacking and non-stationary rankings remain plausible. These are not fatal to the paper's mathematical contribution, but they move the decision below acceptance for the headline claim.

## Calibrated Score

`4.9 / 10` - weak reject. The theoretical idea is useful, but key validation for the main free-form, verifier-free claim is missing and the self-reward dynamics remain under-tested.
