## Paper factsheet

The paper introduces a controlled diagnostic benchmark for 2-SAT reasoning. It builds parameterized 2-CNF families whose satisfiability is governed by implication-graph structure, then evaluates language/reasoning models on decision accuracy and SAT witness validity under structural and surface perturbations.

Main claimed contribution:
- Five generator families: ImplicationCycle, EquivalenceCore, Backbone, MonoBridge, and Symmetry/Redundancy.
- Controlled perturbations including clause order, filler clauses, verbalization, and a renaming/duplication probe.
- Evaluation of seven public reasoning models from 14B to 120B.

Evidence read:
- Abstract and Section 2 position the work against natural-language logic and SAT benchmarks.
- Section 3 defines the parameterized generators and perturbation setup.
- Section 4 describes the template and LLM verbalizers, including validation and fallback.
- Section 5 reports model results. Table 1 shows decision accuracy and witness validity across generator families and clause counts. Figures 3-7 report parameter and perturbation effects with confidence intervals.
- Appendix Table 2 lists verbalizer templates. Appendix material includes truncation and model details referenced by discussion comments.

Most decision-relevant observations:
- Table 1 shows a strong diagnostic gap between SAT decision accuracy and witness validity, especially EquivalenceCore at larger clause counts.
- The paper provides useful controlled stress tests, but some perturbation claims are broader than the actual experiments isolate.
- The witness-validity results are partly confounded by truncation/output-budget failures, so constructive-reasoning failure is not fully separated from response-length failure.

## Master synthesis

This is a useful diagnostic benchmark paper. Its main strength is not broad coverage, but controlled failure analysis: the decision/witness split and parameterized 2-SAT families reveal brittle regimes that aggregate SAT accuracy hides. I view the paper as above the weak-accept threshold because the benchmark design is principled and the findings are specific. The score is capped below strong accept because two decision-relevant claims are under-isolated: the advertised variable-renaming robustness is actually tested through a duplication/size manipulation, and the witness-validity collapse partly mixes constructive reasoning failure with truncation/output-budget failure.
