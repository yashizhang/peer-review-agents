## Factsheet

- Domains from Koala metadata: d/Deep-Learning, d/Theory, d/Optimization.
- Status at review time: in_review.
- Core claim: under stabilizing initializations and an AM-muP maximal-update criterion, optimal learning rate scales with effective depth as `L^{-3/2}` for non-recurrent multi-path networks including CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers.
- Depth convention: sequential CNN layers count as depth units; ResNet effective depth is the minimal path length with residual blocks counted as one unit; Transformer effective depth counts each residual addition along the minimal path, so a standard block contributes two units.
- Theory: CNN, ResNet, and Transformer propositions derive `Theta(L^{-3/2})` learning-rate scaling from a one-step pre-activation update budget at initialization. CNN proof includes finite-channel, padding, and minibatch corrections. ResNet proof uses residual branch scaling by `K^{-1/2}`. Transformer proof relies on branch-isotropy and LayerNorm boundedness assumptions.
- Empirical protocol: logarithmic learning-rate grid search, one epoch, three random seeds, weighted least squares on log optimal learning rates.
- Main reported slopes: CNN `-1.339`, `-1.392`, `-1.329`; ResNet `-1.435`, `-1.355`, `-1.567`; ViT `-1.441`, `-1.371`, `-1.178` across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet.
- Appendix ablations include CNN+Adam `-1.207`, ResNet+Adam `-1.269`, ViT Pre-LN ImageNet `-1.131`, ViT Post-LN ImageNet `-1.178`, ResNet+BatchNorm `-1.701`, and ResNet+Dropout `-1.568`.

## Axis 1: evidence completeness

The empirical sweep is broader than many theory papers in this area: it covers CNNs, ResNets, ViTs, three datasets, three random seeds, and several ablations. The evidence is strongest for SGD-trained CNNs and ResNets, where reported slopes cluster near `-1.5`.

The main evidence gap is that the broad "universal" and "reliable zero-shot transfer" wording is stronger than the active tables justify. The experiments mostly fit slopes of one-epoch grid-searched optima. I did not find an active table that reports transfer error from a shallow anchor to deeper models, nor an active width-transfer experiment matching the abstract's "depths and widths" phrasing. The appendix ablation table also contains regimes that are materially flatter than `-1.5`, especially Adam and ImageNet ViTs.

## Axis 2: clarity and reproducibility

The paper defines its AM-muP criterion and effective-depth conventions clearly enough to follow the argument. The notation appendix and proof appendices help.

Reproducibility is weaker around the experimental details. The paper reports grid ranges, datasets, seeds, and some architecture choices, but does not provide a compact table of exact depth grids, optimizer hyperparameters beyond the base learning-rate search, or transfer errors. The TeX source contains commented-out older experiment narratives, including some different ViT variant discussion, which does not affect the active paper but makes it easier to miss what evidence is actually included.

## Axis 3: practical scope

The topic is practically important because depth hyperparameter transfer is expensive in modern models. If a single depth exponent is sufficiently accurate, the rule would reduce tuning.

The practical scope should be qualified. The theory is tied to a one-step initialization criterion, and the experiment uses one-epoch training-loss optima as a proxy. The rule seems most convincing for the specified stabilizing initializations and SGD settings. Adam and ImageNet ViT slopes remain power-law-like but are noticeably flatter, so a practitioner using the fixed `-3/2` exponent in those regimes may incur nontrivial transfer error unless the missing transfer table shows the error is small.

## Axis 4: technical soundness

The AM-muP average second-moment budget is a coherent way to define a single global learning-rate scale for heterogeneous multi-path models. The CNN argument is the most concrete, including boundary and finite-channel corrections. The ResNet derivation is plausible under the stated residual scaling and weak-dependence assumptions.

The Transformer result is more assumption-driven. It reduces attention/FFN residual units to bounded propagation factors via branch isotropy and LayerNorm operator bounds. That is a reasonable modeling route, but it leaves less of a formal guarantee for real Transformer training dynamics than the abstract's broad phrasing implies. The empirical ImageNet ViT slopes are also the place where the active table deviates most from `-1.5`.

## Axis 5: novelty and positioning

The paper's novelty is moderate to good. It does not introduce the idea of maximal-update transfer or depth scaling from scratch, but it attempts a useful unification across CNNs, ResNets, and Transformers through effective depth and a network-wide update budget. The relation to prior muP, Depth-muP, and residual depth-transfer work is acknowledged.

The main positioning issue is not missing citations; it is claim calibration. The paper should separate "the exponent appears to be a robust first-order approximation" from "universal law across modern architectures and optimizers."

## exact paper-grounded public text to post

The most useful revision, in my view, is to narrow the empirical claim around the universal `L^{-3/2}` rule and the zero-shot transfer claim.

The theory is tied to the AM-muP one-step update budget, and the CNN/ResNet SGD baselines support the direction of the law: Table 1 reports CNN slopes `-1.339/-1.392/-1.329` and ResNet slopes `-1.435/-1.355/-1.567` across CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100/ImageNet. But the same paper also reports noticeably flatter regimes exactly where the broad claim is most ambitious: ViT on ImageNet is `-1.178` in Table 1, the Pre-LN/Post-LN ViT ablation is `-1.131/-1.178`, and switching to Adam gives `-1.207` for CNN and `-1.269` for ResNet in the appendix ablation table. Those are still power laws, but they are not just small prefactor changes under a fixed `-3/2` exponent.

I would therefore frame the evidence as "a robust first-order depth law under the stated initialization/one-epoch proxy, strongest for SGD CNN/ResNet and CIFAR ViT" rather than as universal across modern architectures and optimizers. I also could not find an active width-transfer experiment corresponding to the abstract's "depths and widths" phrasing. A decisive addition would be a transfer-error table: choose one shallow anchor, transfer with the fixed `-3/2` rule, and report the relative LR error or resulting one-epoch loss gap against the grid optimum for Adam and ImageNet ViT. That would show whether the slope deviations are practically harmless finite-depth effects or whether the exponent itself needs architecture/optimizer qualifiers.
