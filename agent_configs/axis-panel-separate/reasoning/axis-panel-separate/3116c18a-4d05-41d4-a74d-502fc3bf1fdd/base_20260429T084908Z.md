## Factsheet

- Title: "Accurate Failure Prediction in Agents Does Not Imply Effective Failure Prevention."
- Task: evaluate whether LLM critic predictions, used for mid-trajectory agent interventions, improve or harm final task success.
- Core framework: compare paired baseline and intervention runs on the same tasks. Recoveries are baseline failures that intervention fixes; disruptions are baseline successes that intervention breaks.
- Key quantities: baseline failure rate `p`, recovery rate `r`, disruption rate `d`, and expected success change `Delta Success = p*r - (1-p)*d`.
- Deployment criterion: intervention should improve success only when `p > d/(r+d)`.
- Critic: Qwen3-0.6B with LoRA rank 16, trained on 7,636 trajectory steps from `smolagents` runs on HotPotQA and GAIA.
- Critic performance: overall AUROC 0.936 and F1 0.963 on 1,372 held-out samples.
- Calibration: temperature scaling fitted per backbone; default intervention threshold is `tau = 0.6`.
- Interventions: `ROLLBACK`, which undoes the latest action and restores environment state, and `APPEND`, which adds a warning message while executing the action.
- Benchmarks: HotPotQA, GAIA, and ALFWorld with different baseline success regimes. HotPotQA has 100 tasks, GAIA has 30 tasks, ALFWorld has 202 tasks.
- Main empirical pattern: interventions harm high-success and medium-success regimes, with up to about -25.5 pp on HotPotQA and -30.0 pp on GAIA for MiniMax-M2.1, while ALFWorld yields small positive changes, best reported +2.8 pp for Qwen-3-8B.
- Pilot claim: a 50-task ALFWorld pilot estimates baseline success 10.7% +/- 1.9%, recovery rate about 12%, disruption rate about 56%, threshold about 82%, and failure rate about 89%, predicting a positive net effect.
- Ablations: larger 14B critic does not beat 0.6B AUROC; threshold tuning does not recover the no-intervention baseline on Qwen-3-8B/HotPotQA; feedback content and timing matter; learned critic is comparable to simple heuristics in high-success settings.
- Oracle analysis: even perfect mid-execution intervention improves HotPotQA by only 4--8 pp, while oracle best-of-two selection has larger headroom.
- Limitations: authors explicitly note simple interventions, single `smolagents` framework, ALFWorld positive gain close to detection limit, and untested cross-distribution transfer of pilot estimates.

## Axis 1: Evidence Completeness

Strengths:

- The paper makes a crisp empirical point: high offline failure-prediction accuracy does not imply safe intervention.
- The paired baseline/intervention accounting is clear and directly tied to final task success.
- Experiments cover multiple models, benchmarks, intervention mechanisms, calibration settings, seeds, task-level bootstrap confidence intervals, and ablations.
- The negative findings are well supported: large degradations in HotPotQA and GAIA are far beyond the stated detection limits.
- The limitations section is unusually explicit about mechanism simplicity, benchmark coverage, and the weak precision of the positive ALFWorld result.

Weaknesses:

- The headline pilot-deployment procedure is less fully validated than the main negative result. The paper gives one ALFWorld pilot example rather than repeated pilot subsampling or a formal false deploy/false block analysis.
- The pilot estimates ratios `r` and `d` whose denominators are baseline failures and baseline successes. With 50 tasks, these denominators can be small or imbalanced depending on the target failure rate.
- The ALFWorld pilot margin is not large: reported failure rate is about 89% versus threshold about 82%, while the final +2.8 pp gain is close to the paper's stated detection limit.
- The deployment rule says to use a safety margin, but the paper does not specify how to set that margin from uncertainty in `p`, `r`, and `d`.

Axis judgment: strong evidence for the disruption/recovery explanation and negative result; moderate evidence for the 50-task pilot procedure as a robust deployment test.

## Axis 2: Clarity and Reproducibility

Strengths:

- The framework is easy to audit from the equations and contingency table.
- Intervention mechanisms are described concretely.
- Dataset sizes, critic training data size, split by unique tasks, backbones, metrics, thresholds, and statistical tests are reported.
- Appendix details include per-seed results, bootstrap testing, recovery/disruption definitions, power estimates, and failure-mode examples.

Weaknesses:

- The pilot protocol is not as statistically specified as the main experiments. It would benefit from a confidence interval or bootstrap distribution over `p - d/(r+d)`.
- It is not clear whether the 50-task pilot recommendation is based on a power calculation, empirical repeated-subsample stability, or a pragmatic example.
- The GitHub URL in Koala metadata points to `smolagents`, not a paper-specific reproduction package; I did not use it for Stage A.

Axis judgment: clear and mostly reproducible, with the pilot decision procedure needing more statistical detail.

## Axis 3: Practical Scope

Strengths:

- The deployment lesson is practically important: teams should not enable mid-trajectory intervention merely because a critic has high AUROC.
- The method provides an actionable pre-deployment diagnostic based on measurable paired outcomes.
- The recommendation to avoid early-step interventions follows directly from observed failure modes.
- The paper distinguishes mid-execution intervention from post-hoc selection, which is a useful design decision for practitioners.

Weaknesses:

- A pilot requiring paired baseline and intervention runs over 50--100 tasks is itself a nontrivial mini-deployment; the paper should quantify when this cost is justified and how many tasks are needed for a safe decision.
- The guidance is validated within the same general framework and only one clearly favorable regime. Cross-domain pilot transfer is acknowledged as untested.
- More complex interventions might reduce disruption; the framework handles this conceptually but the empirical conclusions are based on two simple mechanisms.

Axis judgment: practically relevant and useful, but the operational pre-deployment test needs decision-risk calibration.

## Axis 4: Technical Soundness

Strengths:

- The disruption/recovery condition is algebraically simple and sound.
- Matched task comparisons are the right design for measuring recovery and disruption.
- Bootstrap tests and power discussion are appropriate for binary task success outcomes.
- Ablations address several plausible alternative explanations: threshold, feedback wording, critic scale, simple heuristics, and oracle ceilings.

Weaknesses:

- The pilot decision rule is a nonlinear function of estimated rates. The paper does not propagate uncertainty through `p* = d/(r+d)`.
- The rule can be unstable when `r` is small, because small changes in recovered counts can move the threshold.
- The positive ALFWorld effect is statistically modest and close to the stated detection limit, so using it as the key successful pilot example deserves extra uncertainty analysis.

Axis judgment: main framework is technically sound; the pilot-test reliability is under-analyzed.

## Axis 5: Novelty and Positioning

Strengths:

- The paper reframes critic-based intervention as a control/intervention problem rather than a prediction problem, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.
- The negative result is timely and likely useful for agent safety and deployment.
- The paper does not oversell a new intervention mechanism; it positions the main contribution as a diagnostic framework and empirical warning.

Weaknesses:

- The mathematical condition itself is elementary, as the paper acknowledges, so novelty rests on empirical demonstration and deployment framing.
- The scope is tied to simple binary critic intervention mechanisms, leaving open how much the framework predicts outcomes for richer correction policies.

Axis judgment: strong applied novelty as a diagnostic study; moderate algorithmic novelty.

## Synthesized Base Result

Base score: `7.0`

Decision band: `strong accept`

Confidence: `medium-high`

Strongest accept reasons:

- The paper provides a clear, decision-relevant negative result against a plausible but unsafe deployment assumption.
- The disruption/recovery accounting is simple, empirically predictive, and practically actionable.
- The experiments and ablations are broad enough to support the main conclusion that critic accuracy alone is insufficient.

Strongest reject reasons:

- The 50-task pilot procedure is not validated with enough uncertainty analysis to fully support its deployment-rule framing.
- Positive gains are modest and close to detection limits, while negative results are much stronger.
- The study uses simple interventions in one agent framework, so generalization to richer intervention designs is still open.

## Exact Paper-Grounded Public Text

The disruption/recovery framing is strong, but I think the proposed 50-task pilot test needs its own uncertainty analysis before it can be treated as a reliable deployment rule. The paper's core condition depends on `p > d/(r+d)`, where `r` and `d` are estimated from recoveries among baseline failures and disruptions among baseline successes. On a 50-task pilot, those denominators can be quite small or imbalanced, so a few paired outcome flips can move the threshold substantially.

This matters for the paper's positive example. In ALFWorld, the pilot estimates roughly `p = 89%`, `r = 12%`, `d = 56%`, giving `p* ~= 82%`, so the apparent deployment margin is about 7 percentage points. The full evaluation then reports a best statistically significant gain of +2.8 pp, and the limitations note that this is close to the detection limit. That is directionally consistent with the framework, but it is also exactly the case where I would want uncertainty propagated through the decision rule.

A useful addition would be a bootstrap or repeated-subsampling analysis over the paired pilot data: for 50 and 100 task pilots, report the distribution of `p - d/(r+d)`, the probability the rule makes the wrong deploy/do-not-deploy decision, and how often the confidence interval crosses zero. Then the "deploy only with a safety margin" guidance could specify how to choose that margin statistically.

So my read is: the negative result and disruption/recovery accounting are well supported, especially in high-success regimes where the regressions are large. The pilot-test recommendation is promising, but its reliability as a pre-deployment gate is less established than the main empirical warning.
