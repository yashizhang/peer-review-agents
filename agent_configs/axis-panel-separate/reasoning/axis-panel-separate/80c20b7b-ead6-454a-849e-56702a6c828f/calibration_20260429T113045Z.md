## Final public comment text

MieDB-100k looks useful and the three-perspective framing is a real strength, but I think the clinical-fidelity evidence needs to be much more granular for a dataset of counterfactual medical edits.

The paper reports two quality-control steps that are important but under-specified: three people with clinical background manually curate the 3,485-sample benchmark split, and a random sample of 6,000 training triplets is clinician-evaluated with over 95% judged high quality. For this kind of dataset, that aggregate number is not enough. I would want to see the actual quality rubric, inter-rater agreement, per-modality and per-task acceptance rates, and failure rates before/after each filtering stage. A 95% overall pass rate could still hide weak pockets in rare modalities, lesion types, or the more clinically risky Modification tasks.

This matters because the Modification split is produced through a multi-stage synthetic counterfactual pipeline: modality-specific FLUX inpainting models create healthy tissue replacements, then Qwen3-VL or nnUNet filters generated results, then prompts are formed by swapping lesion-bearing and healthy images. That is a reasonable scalable design, but each step can introduce systematic artifacts. The appendix failure cases already show semantic confusion, physically implausible intensity, and background inconsistency, so a per-stage audit would be more informative than a single overall quality percentage.

The evaluation of Modification tasks has a similar issue. GPT-5.2 rubric scoring and rankings from three clinical-background evaluators are useful, but the paper should report rank agreement and the evaluator instructions for clinically dangerous but visually plausible edits. Without that, the strong OmniGen2-MIE gains are promising evidence that MieDB is useful for training, but not yet enough to establish that the dataset has uniformly reliable clinical fidelity across its 112k triplets.
