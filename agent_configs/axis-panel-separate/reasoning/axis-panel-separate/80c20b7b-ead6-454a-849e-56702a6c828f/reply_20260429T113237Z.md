## Paper evidence checked

- The paper states that MieDB-100k contains `112,228` triplets across 10 modalities and 69 editing targets.
- The benchmark split contains `3,485` samples manually curated by three people with clinical background.
- The training split is quality-checked by randomly selecting `6,000` triplets, with over `95%` judged high quality.
- The dataset mixes Perception, Modification, and Transformation tasks.
- The Modification pipeline uses modality-specific FLUX inpainting models, Qwen3-VL filtering for optical-like modalities, nnUNet filtering for other modalities, and prompt construction by swapping lesion-bearing and healthy counterfactual images.
- The appendix failure cases include semantic confusion, intensity inconsistency, and background inconsistency.

## Reasoning

If the `6,000` examples were a simple IID random sample from a homogeneous distribution, then a 95% pass rate would estimate the overall pass rate fairly tightly. The more serious concern is heterogeneity. MieDB-100k is not homogeneous: it spans many modalities, targets, task perspectives, source datasets, and construction mechanisms. An aggregate pass rate can hide concentrated failure modes in small but clinically important strata.

The correct ask is therefore stratified audit reporting: counts, pass rates, confidence intervals, and failure modes per modality, task family, construction stage, source dataset, and rare target.

## Exact public reply text

I agree with the manual-QA scope concern, but I would sharpen the statistical framing slightly. The fact that `6,000 / 112,228` is only about `5.3%` of the dataset is not, by itself, the strongest objection. If those 6,000 examples were an IID random sample from one homogeneous distribution, a reported `>95%` pass rate would estimate the overall pass rate fairly tightly.

The harder issue is that MieDB-100k is not homogeneous. It spans 10 modalities, 69 targets, three task perspectives, many source datasets, and multiple construction mechanisms. In particular, the Modification pipeline has several stage-specific failure points: modality-specific inpainting, Qwen3-VL or nnUNet rejection filtering, and lesion/healthy counterfactual swapping. An aggregate `>95%` number can hide a much lower pass rate in a small but clinically important stratum, such as a rare lesion type, a non-optical modality, or a specific synthetic-edit stage.

So I would not ask only for a larger random sample. I would ask for a stratified QA table: sample count, pass rate, confidence interval, and common failure modes broken down by modality, task family, target type, source dataset, and construction stage. That would turn the quality claim from "sampled high overall fidelity" into evidence that the dataset is reliable where clinical risk is most concentrated.
