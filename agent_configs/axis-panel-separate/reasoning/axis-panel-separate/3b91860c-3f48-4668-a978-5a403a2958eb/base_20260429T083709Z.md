## Factsheet

- Title: "Learning to Repair Lean Proofs from Compiler Feedback."
- Task: supervised Lean proof repair from an erroneous proof plus compiler feedback, with an additional natural-language diagnosis/fix-suggestion target.
- Dataset: APRIL, 260,125 incorrect Lean proofs derived from 39,492 unique compiled theorems.
- Sources: Herald, Lean Workbook, and NuminaMath-Lean; proofs are retained only if they compile under Lean 4.22.0-rc4.
- Error generation: theorem substitutions, tactic replacements, line-level redaction/modification, and multi-line redaction/modification.
- Largest mutation class: theorem substitutions, reported as 59.5% of generated incorrect proofs.
- Compiler grounding: mutated proofs are retained only when Lean reports an error; the dataset stores error messages and local goal state.
- Explanation/fix target generation: DeepSeek-V3-0324 is used to generate natural-language explanations and fix suggestions. The main method says the prompt includes the original proof, mutated proof, compiler error, and available mutation metadata.
- Fine-tuning input: the model receives prover error, local proof state, and failing proof. The supervised target encourages a diagnostic step before code generation.
- Evaluation: held-out test set of 1,835 erroneous proofs spanning all mutation types; success is Lean compilation under Lean 4.22.0-rc4.
- Main result: APRIL fine-tuning raises Qwen3-4B-Instruct repair accuracy from 1.1% to 27.4% in the joint repair-plus-explanation setting. The same single-shot interface gives Goedel-Prover-V2-32B 26.8%.
- Explanation ablation: removing explanations and training only for repair raises Qwen pass@1 from 27.4% to 31.2%. Explanations from the trained Qwen improve a DeepSeek repair pipeline from 4% success with base-Qwen explanations to 29%.
- Appendix prompt evidence: theorem-mutation explanation prompts explicitly include a metadata "cheatsheet" with intended correct theorem versus substituted incorrect theorem, and tactic-mutation prompts include intended correct line versus current incorrect line. Line and multi-line mutation prompts are restricted to incorrect proof, infoview state, and Lean error for explanation generation, but DeepSeek is also used to produce those errors.
- Authors release dataset/model links in the paper.

## Axis 1: Evidence Completeness

Strengths:

- The paper gives a concrete, large-scale dataset construction pipeline and reports dataset counts, source shares, split strategy, mutation-type distribution, model setup, and evaluation protocol.
- The held-out split is at the original-theorem level, which is an important safeguard because multiple mutations can arise from a single correct proof.
- The main comparison is clearly framed as single-shot repair rather than end-to-end theorem proving with search, reducing the risk of overstating the Goedel-Prover comparison.
- The explanation ablation is useful because it shows a real tradeoff: explanation supervision improves human-readable diagnosis but reduces autonomous pass@1 relative to repair-only training.

Weaknesses:

- The evidence is almost entirely synthetic. The appendix describes failed attempts to create natural or prover-generated repair data, which reinforces that the final distribution may be constrained by what was controllable rather than by naturally occurring Lean development errors.
- There is no evaluation on naturally occurring failed human attempts or failed outputs from a theorem prover/agent. This limits external validity for the intended iterative-proof-development setting.
- The natural-language explanation/fix targets for theorem and tactic mutations are generated with privileged mutation metadata. This makes the explanation-quality evidence less complete because the target generator sees information not available to the model input at training or deployment.
- The paper does not audit whether generated explanations reveal hidden intended theorem/tactic replacements that are not inferable from the compiler state.

Axis judgment: evidence is strong for the existence and utility of a synthetic compiler-feedback repair dataset, but incomplete for claims about realistic debugging distributions and compiler-feedback-grounded natural-language diagnosis.

## Axis 2: Clarity and Reproducibility

Strengths:

- The dataset generation procedure is mostly clear and includes detailed mutation mechanisms, split choices, model training details, and appendix prompts.
- The paper states Lean version, retained data criteria, model families, LoRA setup, training length, and evaluation metric.
- Dataset and model release links are included.

Weaknesses:

- The wording around "diagnosis grounded in the same feedback" is potentially misleading. The fine-tuned model input is compiler feedback plus failing proof, but the annotation model for theorem and tactic explanations receives mutation metadata that is not part of that deployed input.
- Some implementation details remain under-specified for full reproduction, such as exact filtering rates for each mutation strategy, label-generation quality controls, and human or automated validation of explanation correctness.
- The explanation prompts are in the appendix, but their implications for the main claim are not discussed clearly in the main text.

Axis judgment: reproducibility is above average for a dataset paper, but the annotation-information boundary needs clearer presentation.

## Axis 3: Practical Scope

Strengths:

- Lean proof repair from compiler feedback is a practically relevant problem for interactive theorem proving and agentic theorem provers.
- The single-shot repair protocol isolates a component ability that could be used inside larger feedback loops.
- The dataset size and release make the artifact plausibly useful to other groups.

Weaknesses:

- The practical scope is narrower than the introduction might suggest because training and evaluation are on controlled mutations of already-correct proofs rather than real repair traces.
- The single-shot setup is useful as an ablation but does not demonstrate integration into an iterative proof-search system or human-in-the-loop workflow.
- Explanation utility is demonstrated through one downstream DeepSeek augmentation experiment, but not through human debugging or agent-loop outcomes.

Axis judgment: the artifact is practically useful as a component dataset, but deployment relevance remains partially demonstrated.

## Axis 4: Technical Soundness

Strengths:

- Using Lean compilation as the correctness criterion for repair targets and model outputs is technically sound.
- The original-theorem split and theorem-name anonymization are reasonable anti-leakage decisions.
- The comparison against base and prover-specialized models under the same single-shot interface is technically fair for the stated component evaluation.

Weaknesses:

- The explanation/fix suggestion labels for theorem and tactic mutations are generated with oracle mutation metadata. This creates a target-generation mismatch: the model is trained to imitate explanations produced with information that is not available in the model prompt.
- Because theorem substitution is the largest mutation class, this mismatch affects a substantial fraction of the dataset.
- For line and multi-line mutations, the same model family is used to create the erroneous proof and later produce explanation/fix targets. This does not invalidate the verified repair target, but it could bias natural-language diagnoses toward model-specific artifacts.
- The paper does not disentangle gains from learning compiler-feedback interpretation versus learning mutation-family priors.

Axis judgment: repair-target correctness is sound, but the diagnosis-supervision claim is technically weaker than the compilation-based repair claim.

## Axis 5: Novelty and Positioning

Strengths:

- The paper addresses an under-served target for Lean learning systems: supervised repair from failed attempts rather than only final correct proofs.
- Large-scale paired erroneous/correct proofs with compiler feedback and explanations are a valuable contribution relative to proof corpora dominated by successful proofs.
- The paper positions its single-shot repair results carefully with respect to systems normally used with search or iteration.

Weaknesses:

- Synthetic mutation of correct artifacts is a natural and somewhat expected way to build this dataset, so the novelty rests more on scale, engineering, and artifact usefulness than on a new learning algorithm.
- The explanation component is less novel if its labels partly encode hidden mutation metadata rather than diagnoses derived from compiler feedback alone.

Axis judgment: novelty is moderate-to-strong as a dataset contribution, more modest as a modeling contribution.

## Synthesized Base Result

Base score: `6.4`

Decision band: `weak accept`

Confidence: `medium`

Strongest accept reasons:

- The paper introduces a sizable, well-scoped Lean repair dataset that fills a real gap in current proof-training corpora.
- Repairs are evaluated by actual Lean compilation, and the results show large single-shot repair gains after supervised fine-tuning.
- The authors are appropriately cautious about single-shot versus end-to-end proof-search comparisons.

Strongest reject reasons:

- The natural-language diagnostic supervision is partly generated with privileged mutation metadata, so the paper's "grounded in compiler feedback" explanation claim is not fully established.
- Synthetic controlled mutations are not evaluated against natural human/prover repair traces, limiting external validity.
- The work is more of a dataset/engineering contribution than a broadly new learning method.

## Exact Paper-Grounded Public Text

One concern I have is about the boundary between compiler-feedback-grounded diagnosis and oracle-aware label generation. The paper says the fine-tuning input consists of the prover error, local proof state, and failing proof, but the explanation/fix labels are generated from the original proof, mutated proof, compiler error, and available mutation metadata. In the appendix prompts, theorem-mutation labels receive a cheatsheet with the intended theorem versus the substituted theorem, and tactic-mutation labels receive the intended line versus the current incorrect line; theorem substitutions are also the largest mutation class at 59.5%.

This does not undermine the verified repair-target result: the corrected proof is still checked by Lean, the split is at the original-theorem level, and the single-shot repair gains are meaningful. But it weakens the interpretation that the natural-language diagnoses are grounded only in the compiler feedback available to a deployed repair model. The explanation ablation also makes this boundary important: repair-only training improves Qwen pass@1 from 27.4% to 31.2%, while explanation-trained outputs are argued to help downstream DeepSeek repair.

I would find the diagnostic contribution stronger with either an explanation/fix-label generation ablation restricted to the deployed inputs, or an audit reporting how often labels reveal the hidden intended theorem/tactic rather than something inferable from Lean's error and proof state. Without that, I read APRIL as a useful synthetic repair dataset, but the explanation/diagnosis part is less established than the compilation-based repair result.
