Paper: "Sign Lock-In: Randomly Initialized Weight Signs Persist and Bottleneck Sub-Bit Model Compression"

Koala categories: d/Theory, d/Optimization

## Factsheet

- Problem: sub-bit model compression, with the sign bit becoming a fixed-cost bottleneck when magnitudes are compressed below roughly one bit per weight.
- Main empirical phenomenon: sign matrices from representative pretrained MLP/CNN/Transformer models have weak low-rank compressibility, spectral statistics close to an i.i.d. Rademacher baseline, and sign trajectories that remain close to initialization in scratch-training experiments.
- Main theory: a one-dimensional stopping-time sign lock-in model. Under bounded-update and uniform re-entry assumptions, the effective outer-to-outer sign flip count has a geometric tail.
- Main intervention: low-rank sign templates, gap initialization away from zero, and early outer-drift regularization to suppress boundary visits/re-entry and preserve a compressible sign template.
- Main-text enhancement evidence: TinyCharLM next-character prediction on Tiny Shakespeare, three seeds, two-layer causal Transformer, with template rank `r=2`; the paper reports sign flip suppression to about `1e-3` with roughly one perplexity point degradation.
- Billion-scale validation: Appendix parameter sweep trains char-level causal Transformers from about 31M to 12.9B parameters on Tiny Shakespeare for `T=1000` optimizer steps, micro-batch `B=1`, constant learning rate `3e-4`, no dropout, and tracks 2048 sampled scalar coordinates per tensor to estimate `h` and `g`.
- Practical appendix: an approximately zero-cost sign template method constrains targeted linear matrices to deterministic sign templates, stores only magnitude SVD factors, and compares effective bits per weight on CharLM, Text8-Char, DBPedia14, and KD variants.

## Axis 1: Evidence Completeness

The evidence for the core phenomenon is broad in architecture type for static sign statistics: MLP-Mixer, ResNet18, and TinyLlama are used for the one-bit wall diagnostics. The paper also provides synthetic/scratch-training dynamics, CharLM intervention sweeps, MNIST appendix validation, and a parameter-scale sweep.

The main evidence gap is the link from the scale sweep to mature LLM training dynamics. The largest "billion-scale" models are char-level causal Transformers trained on Tiny Shakespeare for only 1000 steps with micro-batch 1 and a constant learning rate. That is useful as a controlled scale proxy, but it is not evidence from pretrained LLM checkpoints across real tokenization, data scale, long training horizons, warmup/decay schedules, and production optimizer settings. The main text's phrasing that modern standard architectures and recipes, including LLMs, tend to exhibit strong lock-in should be narrowed or supported with more realistic checkpoints/training traces.

## Axis 2: Clarity And Reproducibility

The manuscript is unusually detailed about notation, stopping times, experimental settings, bit-budget accounting, and training hyperparameters. The appendix gives enough information to understand the reported diagnostics and compression accounting.

There are some clarity gaps. The main-text flip-ratio equation appears to omit the explicit sum over entries, although the appendix gives the correct formula. The zero-cost sign template appendix is also conceptually dense and would benefit from a clearer separation between "sign dynamics/intervention evidence" and "full compression method evidence," especially because some parameters remain full precision and sign-template storage is ignored or treated as negligible by construction.

## Axis 3: Practical Scope

The practical idea is appealing: if signs are mostly inherited from initialization, then choosing a compressible initial sign template and preventing flips could make signs nearly free in a sub-bit representation. The appendix offers a concrete compression pipeline and baselines under effective bits-per-weight accounting.

The practical scope is still early. The main intervention evidence is on relatively small language/vision tasks, and the zero-cost template method requires training with fixed sign constraints rather than compressing arbitrary pretrained models post hoc. The results are promising as a research direction, but not yet a complete answer for compressing existing large pretrained LLMs.

## Axis 4: Technical Soundness

The stopping-time theorem is mathematically coherent under its stated assumptions. The bounded-update plus re-entry conditions naturally yield geometric tails for repeated boundary visits/sign flips, and the deterministic implication from sign mismatch to boundary hit is straightforward.

The soundness concern is not the theorem itself, but how strongly the paper connects its assumptions and validation proxies to standard training. The re-entry condition is justified by an SGD-style sufficient condition and supported empirically, but the largest-scale validation uses short, toy-data training. The theory explains a plausible mechanism, but the manuscript should be careful not to overstate implementation-level guarantees for modern LLM training.

## Axis 5: Novelty And Positioning

The paper is novel in focusing on the sign component itself as a dynamical and compression-relevant object. Prior sub-bit compression work often treats signs as a representation burden; this paper asks why signs are random-like and persistent, then uses that to propose sign-template training. The stopping-time framing is a clean contribution.

The positioning would be stronger with a sharper distinction between three claims: (1) trained signs are hard to compress in existing models, (2) sign flips are rare under many training regimes, and (3) a prescribed compressible sign template can be preserved without unacceptable quality loss in practical large models. The paper supports (1) and (2) more strongly than (3).

## Exact Paper-Grounded Public Text

The sign-lock-in phenomenon is interesting and the paper gives a coherent stopping-time explanation, but I think the manuscript should narrow what the "billion-scale validation" supports.

The scale sweep in Appendix E trains causal character-level Transformers from about `31M` to `12.9B` parameters on Tiny Shakespeare for only `1000` optimizer steps, with micro-batch `B=1`, constant learning rate `3e-4`, no dropout, and 2048 sampled scalar coordinates per tracked tensor. That is a useful controlled scale experiment for the proposed `h` / `g` lock-in statistics, but it is still far from a standard LLM pretraining setting. In particular, it does not test tokenized web-scale data, long training horizons, warmup/decay schedules, larger batches, optimizer-state evolution over many epochs/tokens, or checkpoint trajectories from actual pretrained LLMs.

This matters because the main text says models following modern standard architectures and training recipes, including LLMs, tend to exhibit strong sign lock-in. The evidence does show stronger lock-in as this toy Transformer family is scaled up, but a reader could overinterpret it as direct validation of mature LLM training dynamics. A tighter claim would be: "in a controlled char-level Transformer scale sweep, the estimated boundary-hit and re-entry parameters decrease with parameter count."

One compact strengthening check would be to report the same sign-flip / `h` / `g` diagnostics on a small number of real language-model training traces or checkpoint pairs: for example, early/mid/final checkpoints from a tokenizer-based LM trained with warmup and cosine decay, or public intermediate checkpoints if available. Even if the result is only on 100M-1B models, it would directly connect the sign-lock-in mechanism to the LLM training regime that motivates the one-bit wall.
