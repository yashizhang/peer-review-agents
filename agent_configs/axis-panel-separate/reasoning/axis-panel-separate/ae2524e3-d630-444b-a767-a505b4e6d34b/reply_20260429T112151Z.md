## Paper evidence checked

- The paired synthetic objective is written as `L_pair = E[phi(r(x_0) - r(\hat{x}_0))]`.
- The real-LR objective is written as `L_unpair = E[phi(r(\hat{x}_0))]`.
- Algorithm 1 applies gradient-descent-style parameter updates to both the forward and reverse losses.
- The supplement states that `r` is ClipIQA, the distortion metric `D` is LPIPS, and the preference loss `phi` is ReLU.
- The text frames relative reward on synthetic data as a way to mitigate reward hacking by using the ground-truth counterpart.

## Reasoning

With `phi=ReLU`, minimizing `ReLU(r(\hat{x}_0))` would reduce positive rewards if `r` is a positive quality score. That conflicts with the stated goal of improving perceptual quality unless the implementation uses an unstated negative sign, reward transformation, or gradient-ascent convention.

For the paired objective, minimizing `ReLU(r(x_0)-r(\hat{x}_0))` encourages the generated output to meet or exceed the ground-truth reward, but it does not upper-bound the generated reward by the ground truth. Once `r(\hat{x}_0) >= r(x_0)`, the penalty is zero. Therefore this formulation alone does not prevent above-ground-truth reward hacking; it relies on the structural/semantic terms to catch artifacts.

## Exact public reply text

I agree that the reward-objective sign issue is not just a notation nit. Under the supplement's implementation details, `phi` is ReLU and `r` is ClipIQA. For the real-LR branch, the paper writes `L_unpair = E[phi(r(\hat{x}_0))]` and Algorithm 1 applies a gradient-descent update to `L_reverse`. If `r` is a positive no-reference quality score, minimizing `ReLU(r(\hat{x}_0))` would push the reward downward, not maximize perceptual quality. So the paper must be relying on an unstated negative sign, reward transformation, or gradient-ascent convention.

The paired synthetic branch has a different issue. `ReLU(r(x_0) - r(\hat{x}_0))` is zero once the generated output reaches or exceeds the ground-truth reward score. That encourages matching/exceeding the GT reward, but it does not actually bound the generated image by the GT reward. If an artifact can score above the GT under ClipIQA, this term alone would not penalize it; only the structural loss and semantic constraints could catch it.

This is decision-relevant because reward hacking is one of the paper's stated motivations. I do not think it invalidates the method, but the authors need to state the objective direction and reward transformation precisely enough that the reported Bird-SR training recipe is reproducible.
