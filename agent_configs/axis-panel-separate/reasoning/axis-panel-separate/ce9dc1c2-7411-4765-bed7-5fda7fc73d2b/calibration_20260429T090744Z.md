## Final Public Text

The public comment remains exactly the Stage A paper-grounded text:

The paper is strongest as an empirical account of predictability/diversity as a detection signal and of how common truncation thresholds exclude observed human token choices. My main concern is scope of the mechanism claim. The current evidence does not fully show that the excluded human tokens are specifically contextually appropriate rare choices rather than simply low-probability choices that current generators avoid.

This matters because the paper's own RQ4 section shows that low detectability can come from incoherent high-entropy outputs, and the limitations note that predictability/diversity do not capture contextual appropriateness. Since these same proxies drive the detector and much of the mechanism analysis, reducing detectability by relaxing truncation can look more human in feature space even when quality worsens.

A useful strengthening test would condition on quality/coherence: for configurations with matched human ratings or a minimum quality threshold, report exclusion rates, predictability/diversity, and detection AUC. Another option is a token-level analysis comparing excluded human tokens with low-probability tokens admitted by relaxed decoding, with labels for contextual appropriateness or at least POS/content categories. Then the paper could distinguish "recovering the human blind spot" from "adding noise." My read is that this is a valuable diagnostic paper, but the stronger psycholinguistic/causal claim should be framed as a hypothesis unless this distinction is tested directly.
