## Factsheet

- Main claim: likelihood-based decoding strategies such as top-k, top-p, contrastive search, and beam search exclude a nontrivial set of human-selected tokens, especially statistically rare but communicatively useful choices; this exclusion contributes to machine-text detectability.
- Scale of study: more than 1.8 million generated texts, eight language models, five decoding strategies, and 53 hyperparameter configurations.
- Human data: 5,261 English examples from BookCorpus, WikiText, and WikiNews.
- Model coverage: open-source models including LLaMA 3, Mistral 3, Qwen 2, Falcon 2, Deepseek, GPT2-XL, plus proprietary GPT-3.5-turbo and Claude-3-Haiku; appendix analyses also include Mamba and RWKV variants.
- Metrics: predictability is average log-likelihood under OPT-2.7B; lexical diversity is a product of unique-to-total 2-gram, 3-gram, and 4-gram ratios.
- Detection setup: simple classifiers using only predictability and lexical diversity, with 80-20 stratified splits.
- Major empirical results:
  - Top-k exclusion rates average 18.1% at k=10, 13.0% at k=20, and 8.1% at k=50.
  - Top-p exclusion rates average 15.6% at p=0.8, 8.0% at p=0.9, and 3.9% at p=0.95.
  - Predictability/diversity classifiers reach AUC-ROC 0.969 to 0.976 on aggregate.
  - Beam search is most detectable at about 0.997 AUC-ROC; top-k and top-p are less detectable but still high.
  - Relaxing truncation reduces detectability, e.g. top-k from k=1 to k=15 lowers AUC-ROC from 0.988 to 0.924.
  - Human quality ratings from a small external public evaluation show weak, nonsignificant correlation with estimated machine probability, and low-detectability outputs can be incoherent.
- Stated limitations: predictability/diversity do not capture contextual appropriateness; intent-driven selection is theoretical; scope is English, three domains, segments up to 256 tokens; exclusion-rate analysis relies on five open-source models.

## Axis 1: Evidence Completeness

The paper provides broad quantitative coverage for the descriptive phenomenon. The exclusion-rate tables make the blind spot measurable under common top-k and top-p thresholds, and the classifier results show that two simple statistics separate human and machine text across many model/strategy settings.

The weaker part is evidence tying the excluded tokens to contextual appropriateness rather than to low probability alone. The appendix POS analysis is useful because content words, numerals, modifiers, and domain-specific terms have high exclusion rates; however, the paper does not directly validate that the admitted low-probability alternatives produced by relaxed decoding are appropriate in context. The RQ4 quality section explicitly shows why this matters: lower detectability can come from incoherent high-entropy outputs.

Evidence completeness judgment: strong for the descriptive distributional mismatch; incomplete for the stronger causal/psycholinguistic interpretation.

## Axis 2: Clarity and Reproducibility

The paper is clearly structured around four research questions, and the mathematical setup makes the exclusion-rate estimate explicit. Dataset sizes, model families, decoding configurations, reference model, classifier families, and key hyperparameters are all described in enough detail to understand the experiment. The abstract includes a code/data link.

The biggest clarity issue is terminology around "human-like" and "contextually appropriate." The paper acknowledges these are not directly measured by predictability/diversity, but the headline framing sometimes reads as if the appropriateness mechanism is established rather than inferred. It would be clearer to separate "tokens selected by humans and excluded by truncation" from "appropriate rare choices" unless a contextual annotation or matched-quality analysis supports the latter.

Clarity/reproducibility judgment: generally clear and reproducible at the metric level; conceptual scope needs tighter wording.

## Axis 3: Practical Scope

The practical scope is relevant: decoding choices are widely used, machine-text detectability matters, and the paper gives an actionable warning that lower detector confidence is not equivalent to higher quality. The quality-detectability dissociation is especially useful because it prevents the paper from being read as an evasion recipe.

Scope limitations are meaningful. The analysis is English-only, short-context, and based on three text domains. It also studies a binary human-vs-machine setting, leaving collaborative writing and post-edited text outside scope.

Practical scope judgment: useful diagnostic contribution with moderate generalization limits.

## Axis 4: Technical Soundness

The exclusion-rate analysis is technically sound as a measurement of where observed human tokens fall under model-ranked truncation sets. The classifier results are also technically straightforward and convincing as evidence of a low-dimensional detection signal.

The main technical concern is construct validity. The same two proxies, predictability and lexical diversity, drive the detection analysis and much of the mechanistic interpretation. The paper itself notes that high diversity can be either genuine lexical richness or incoherent randomness. Because permissive decoding can reduce detectability by admitting inappropriate low-probability tokens, the present analysis does not fully distinguish recovering a human blind spot from adding noise. A stronger technical test would condition on human-rated quality/coherence or use contextual annotation of excluded tokens and generated admitted tokens.

Technical soundness judgment: empirical measurements are credible; causal interpretation should be narrowed or backed by quality-matched/token-level appropriateness tests.

## Axis 5: Novelty and Positioning

The paper positions itself as explaining why detectors work rather than proposing another detector. Framing decoding truncation as a blind spot and quantifying human-token exclusion across strategies is a useful synthesis. The link to detectability, POS composition, and quality tradeoffs makes the paper more than a restatement of known degeneration and perplexity observations.

The novelty depends on the strength of the mechanism claim. The safest contribution is the empirical diagnostic: common decoding policies systematically exclude some human-selected content-bearing tokens, and this exclusion correlates with a simple predictability/diversity detection signal. The stronger claim that these excluded tokens are specifically human-like contextual choices is plausible but less directly demonstrated.

Novelty judgment: moderate to strong empirical framing, with some overreach in interpretation.

## Exact Public Text I Would Post

The paper is strongest as an empirical account of predictability/diversity as a detection signal and of how common truncation thresholds exclude observed human token choices. My main concern is scope of the mechanism claim. The current evidence does not fully show that the excluded human tokens are specifically contextually appropriate rare choices rather than simply low-probability choices that current generators avoid.

This matters because the paper's own RQ4 section shows that low detectability can come from incoherent high-entropy outputs, and the limitations note that predictability/diversity do not capture contextual appropriateness. Since these same proxies drive the detector and much of the mechanism analysis, reducing detectability by relaxing truncation can look more human in feature space even when quality worsens.

A useful strengthening test would condition on quality/coherence: for configurations with matched human ratings or a minimum quality threshold, report exclusion rates, predictability/diversity, and detection AUC. Another option is a token-level analysis comparing excluded human tokens with low-probability tokens admitted by relaxed decoding, with labels for contextual appropriateness or at least POS/content categories. Then the paper could distinguish "recovering the human blind spot" from "adding noise." My read is that this is a valuable diagnostic paper, but the stronger psycholinguistic/causal claim should be framed as a hypothesis unless this distinction is tested directly.
