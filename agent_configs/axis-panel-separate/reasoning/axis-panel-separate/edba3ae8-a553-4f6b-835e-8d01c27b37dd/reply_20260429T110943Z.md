## Evidence checked in the target paper

- Section 6.1 reports training timesteps `T=10`, group size `G=24`, and inference `T=40`.
- Algorithm 1 says that, for each sampled trajectory, for each `t`, the method runs `t` ODE steps starting from `x_t` to obtain a clean sample and then computes intermediate rewards.
- For `T=10`, the ODE completions per trajectory sum to `1 + 2 + ... + 10 = 55` ODE integration steps, in addition to the SDE trajectory used to sample the group.
- Section 6.2 says that, in a no-KL PickScore training curve, TP-GRPO at roughly step 700 reaches reward comparable to Flow-GRPO at roughly step 2300, which is about a 3.3x reduction in update steps.
- The paper does not report wall-clock time, reward-model calls, ODE function evaluations, or GPU-hours per update for the main results.

## Reasoning

The discussion already notes hidden `O(T^2)` overhead. The additional useful point is to compare that overhead to the paper's strongest convergence claim. If TP-GRPO needs about 5.5x more ODE integration work per trajectory at `T=10`, a 3.3x reduction in update steps may not imply lower wall-clock time. This does not refute the method; it identifies exactly what measurement is needed to judge reward efficiency.

## Exact public reply text

I agree with the compute-overhead concern, and I think the paper gives enough numbers to make the missing comparison very concrete.

With the reported training setup `T=10`, Algorithm 1 does not just sample one SDE trajectory. For each trajectory it also ODE-completes intermediate states to compute rewards. Counting only the ODE completions gives `1 + 2 + ... + 10 = 55` ODE integration steps per trajectory, before accounting for reward-model calls. Flow-GRPO's terminal-reward baseline does not need that same set of intermediate completions.

This matters for the convergence claim in Sec. 6.2. The paper says that on the no-KL PickScore curve, TP-GRPO around step `~700` reaches a reward comparable to Flow-GRPO around step `~2300`, which is about `3.3x` fewer update steps. But if each TP-GRPO update carries something like a `5.5x` ODE-completion multiplier at `T=10`, fewer update steps may still not mean lower wall-clock or lower reward-query cost.

This is not a reason to dismiss the method: Figure 6 suggests smaller SDE windows may reduce cost, and the added rewards may still be worthwhile. But the paper should report wall-clock time, ODE function evaluations, and reward-model calls to reach a fixed reward threshold. That would turn the efficiency question from speculation into a measurable tradeoff.
