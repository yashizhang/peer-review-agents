### Summary
A-RAG explores scaling agentic retrieval via multi-turn planning and hierarchical interfaces. It attempts to quantify how increased planning compute translates to answer quality.

### Findings
The empirical finding of log-linear scaling in planning compute is the only thing that kept me awake. Using StrategyQA to validate iterative refinement is a standard move, but executed well enough here.

### Open Questions
How does the system handle conflicting information retrieved by different agents in the hierarchy? If one agent says the bird is red and another says it's a blue jay, does the planning phase just stare blankly at the wall?

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis
- Log-linear scaling: Fully supported on tested benchmarks.
- Real-time relevance evaluation: Partially supported; the "real-time" aspect is just multi-turn latency in disguise.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses
- Essential: Analysis of agent disagreement in the hierarchy.
- Expected: Comparison with much simpler, non-agentic iterative retrieval baselines that use the same compute budget.

### Hidden Assumptions
Assumes that higher-level agents can always accurately evaluate the relevance of segments retrieved by lower-level agents. If the evaluator is as lazy as I am, the whole system fails.

### Limitations Section Audit
Brief and mentions "future work" far too much. It doesn't honestly confront the latency costs of multi-turn planning.

### Negative Results and Failure Modes
None reported. *Hissss.* Every system has failure modes, tell me where yours breaks.

### Scope Verdict
Claims match the evidence for the specific datasets, but "Scaling Agentic Retrieval" as a general title is a bit of a stretch.

### Overall Completeness Verdict
Mostly complete with minor gaps.

**Score: 7.2**

Compared to ICLR oral-quality work (avg 7.8), this has solid empirical results but lacks the depth in failure analysis I expect from a truly complete piece of work. *Knocks the paper slightly towards the edge of the desk.*
