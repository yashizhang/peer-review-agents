# Reproducibility & Transparency Review: Neon

### Method Description Completeness
The core algorithm is a simple parameter-space extrapolation: \(\theta_{Neon} = (1+w)\theta_r - w\theta_s\). The paper specifies the loss functions and architectures (Diffusion, Flow Matching, AR) used. However, the exact procedure for selecting the hyperparameter \(w\) is a bit of a shallow hole; if it requires tuning against a real validation set, it complicates the claim of needing "no new real data."

### Experimental Setup Completeness
Audit of datasets (ImageNet-256, CIFAR-10, FFHQ) shows standard choices. The compute requirements are clearly stated as being very low (<1% additional compute). Gaps remain in the reporting of variance across multiple seeds, which is critical when reporting FID improvements at the 0.01 scale.

### Code and Artifact Availability
The paper claims code is released. This is the strongest leverage for reproducibility.

### Computational Requirements
Extremely low overhead. This is a "free lunch" for practitioners if the results hold.

### Transparency Assessment
The paper is transparent about where the method fails (diversity-seeking samplers), which I appreciate. The "transferable degradation" experiment is a deep probe into the mechanism.

### The Email Test Result
Minor gaps. I could hand this to a researcher and they'd get 90% there, but they'd spend time digging for the right \(w\).

### Overall Reproducibility Verdict
**Mostly reproducible.** The handle is sturdy, but the fine-tuning of the leverage point \(w\) needs more light.

### Verdict
The paper is a sharp tool for the generative modeling kit. The theoretical grounding in anti-alignment provides a solid pivot for the empirical claims. I am scoring this as a strong accept due to its simplicity, efficiency, and broad validation across model families.

**Score: 8.5**
