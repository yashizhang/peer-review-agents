# Verdict for GTPO AND GRPO-S: TOKEN AND SEQUENCE-LEVEL REWARD SHAPING WITH POLICY ENTROPY

### Summary
This paper proposes GTPO and GRPO-S, entropy-weighted variants of GRPO for LLM reasoning. The goal is to improve fine-grained credit assignment by using policy entropy to reshape rewards. While the idea is interesting, the theoretical grounding is shaky and the experimental suite lacks critical controls.

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis
- **Entropy as Credit Assignment**: [Partially supported] - While they show benchmark gains, they don't prove entropy actually identifies pivotal steps.
- **Same Global Optimum as DAPO**: [Overclaimed] - Theorem 2.4 claims the shaped objective shares the same optimum, but the proof only covers reward mass conservation, which is insufficient.
- **Performance Gains on AIME/MATH**: [Fully supported] - Reported gains over GRPO are present, though statistical significance on 30-problem sets like AIME is always a concern.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses
- **Ablation of Entropy Weighting**: [Essential] - Missing a comparison with other weighting schemes (e.g., random, gradient-norm) to prove entropy is the special sauce.
- **Token-Level Validation**: [Expected] - No direct evidence that high-entropy tokens are actually the pivotal reasoning junctures.

### Hidden Assumptions
- Assumes that model uncertainty (entropy) is a reliable proxy for reasoning importance, ignoring that it could also signal confusion or hallucination.

### Limitations Section Audit
- [Specific] - Acknowledges the sensitivity of the entropy bonus hyperparameter.
- [Incomplete] - Fails to discuss the theoretical gap between reward redistribution and optimization landscape preservation.

### Scope Verdict
The empirical results are promising for specialized reasoning tasks, but the conceptual framing of "solved credit assignment" is far from complete.

### Overall Completeness Verdict
**Significant gaps in theoretical justification and experimental control.**

1. Theorem 2.4 is a major technical overreach that undermines the theoretical contribution.
